# School Board Research — PEARLS Review Guide

This is the canonical guide for conducting a PEARLS research evaluation on School Board Research. Every review must follow this process to ensure consistency.

---

## Pre-Review Checklist

Before scoring, confirm:
1. Full text obtained (not abstract only)
2. Full APA citation recorded including DOI or stable URL
3. Study type identified (Meta-Analysis / RCT / Longitudinal / Survey Study / Case Study / Policy Analysis / Literature Review)
4. Source confirmed as peer-reviewed OR credentialed policy institution (RAND, Brookings, IES, McREL, AASA, NSBA)

---

## Default Assumption

**Score 1 for every criterion** unless specific evidence in the paper warrants a higher score. State the evidence for every score above 1. This is scientific hygiene, not pessimism.

---

## PEARLS Scoring Criteria

### P — Participants (1–5)
*Who was studied?*

Ask: What is n? How were participants selected? Who is the target population? Do participants match it? Are demographics reported?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Very small (<30) or non-representative with no rationale. Participants clearly mismatch intended application. |
| 2 | Small or narrow demographics; limitations acknowledged but not adequately addressed. |
| 3 | Adequate for study type (qualitative n=20 is fine for case study) with noted limitations in generalizability. |
| 4 | Large sample, good diversity; some representativeness limitations acknowledged. |
| 5 | Large, diverse, well-matched to population. Strong demographic/contextual relevance. Sampling rationale clearly stated. |

---

### E — Execution (1–5)
*How was it conducted?*

Ask: What study design? (RCT, QED, longitudinal, cross-sectional, survey, case study, meta-analysis?) Methods clearly described and replicable? Obvious flaws? Does the design support the claims being made?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Methods unclear, poorly explained, or flawed. No credible evidence possible from this design. |
| 2 | Methods present but key limitations not addressed. Design ill-suited to the research question. |
| 3 | Sound for the study type but not designed to establish causal impact. |
| 4 | Strong methodology; minor limitations; replicable. |
| 5 | Gold standard for study type (RCT, QED, rigorous longitudinal). Design capable of supporting causal claims if made. |

---

### A — Analysis (1–5)
*What do the conclusions actually claim?*

Ask: Are conclusions supported by data? Correlation vs. causation distinguished? Statistical claims caveated? Alternate explanations acknowledged? Limitations explicit?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Conclusions overreach. Causation claimed without evidence. No distinction from correlation. |
| 2 | Some conclusions supported; others stretch evidence. Limited limitation discussion. |
| 3 | Data reasonably interpreted; findings match method; some claims may stretch evidence. |
| 4 | Careful analysis; appropriate caveats; alternate explanations considered. |
| 5 | Fully transparent. Correlation/causation distinctions explicit. Limitations and alternatives clearly acknowledged. |

---

### R — Relevance (1–5)
*Does it apply here, now?*

Ask: When was data collected? Published when? Is the context (geography, grade levels, policy environment) transferable to current U.S. school board governance? Could this be used in a real board conversation today?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Outdated (>15 years and field has shifted) or context totally irrelevant to U.S. school board governance. |
| 2 | Old but still cited; context partially relevant. |
| 3 | Somewhat applicable; topic timely but lacks direct transferability. |
| 4 | Recent and relevant; context roughly matches; can inform practice with appropriate translation. |
| 5 | Highly timely AND directly applicable to school board governance. Could anchor a board retreat or policy conversation today. |

---

### L — Links (1–5)
*Who funded it?*

Ask: Who funded the research? Do funders have financial, ideological, or business interests in the outcome? Conflicts of interest disclosed? Peer-reviewed or credentialed institution?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Funded by a clearly conflicted source (vendor, advocacy org) with no independence safeguards. |
| 2 | Funders have interests in outcome; some transparency but limited independence. |
| 3 | Funding unclear or undisclosed but no obvious conflict apparent. |
| 4 | Independent funding with disclosure; or peer-reviewed with standard COI statement. |
| 5 | Fully transparent, independent funding (NSF, IES, nonpartisan foundations). Authors declare no conflicts. |

---

### S — Scope (1–5)
*How comprehensive is it?*

Ask: How many variables examined? Situated in broader theory? Does it acknowledge what it doesn't explain? Generalizable or narrow?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 1 | Narrow and shallow — one variable, one context, no limitations acknowledged. |
| 2 | Addresses immediate question but misses obvious contextual factors. |
| 3 | Offers depth or breadth but not both. Examines one dimension well. |
| 4 | Good breadth and depth; some cross-dimensional analysis. |
| 5 | Comprehensive, reflective — sufficient breadth, depth, and complexity. Acknowledges what it does and doesn't explain. |

---

## Score Interpretation

| Total | Band | Use |
|-------|------|-----|
| 6–11 | Not usable | Citation only with explicit limitation note. Do not use to inform governance decisions. |
| 12–19 | Use with significant caveats | Useful for hypothesis generation and illustrating concepts. Not for decision support alone. |
| 20–25 | Usable with caveats | Can inform governance discussions; name the caveats when citing. |
| 26–30 | High-confidence evidence | Suitable for anchoring governance policy decisions. |

---

## Required Sections in Every Published Review

Each HTML review page must include these sections in order:

1. **Full APA citation** with DOI link (or stable URL if no DOI)
2. **Study type badge** (Meta-Analysis, RCT, Longitudinal, Survey Study, Case Study, Policy Analysis, Literature Review)
3. **PEARLS score hero** — total score, score band, one-sentence interpretation
4. **One-sentence finding** in plain language
5. **PEARLS scorecard table** — criterion, score (1–5), evidence for that score
6. **Total score row** with interpretation band
7. **Key findings** (2–4 bullets of what the data actually shows — distinct from author conclusions)
8. **Limitations** — what the paper itself acknowledges, plus any limitations the review identifies
9. **Practitioner implications** — specific to school board members and governance practitioners
10. **Editorial synthesis** — What does this mean for boards? Apply the worldview that governance exists to benefit children. Note if this study supports or complicates that lens.

---

## Editorial Voice Notes

- Distinguish between what the *data* shows and what the *authors* claim. These sometimes differ.
- Note when a widely cited study has limitations that the governance field tends to overlook.
- Never assert causation the study doesn't support.
- The "What this means for boards" section is explicitly opinionated — frame it in terms of student benefit.
- If a study scores below 20, say so plainly. Don't soften it because the study is popular.
