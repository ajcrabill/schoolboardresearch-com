Core Finding: Superintendent turnover is associated with a small but consistent decline in student achievement — approximately −0.015 standard deviation units on average. The negative effects are not uniform: urban districts, larger school systems, and districts serving students in concentrated poverty bear the greatest achievement losses following leadership transitions. Critically, the authors find that much of the harm is traceable not to the act of transition itself, but to the underlying district instability that drove the superintendent's departure in the first place.
Methodology: The study uses an event study difference-in-differences design applied to statewide administrative data from Florida and Texas covering nine school years (2009–10 through 2017–18). This quasi-experimental approach compares achievement trajectories in districts that experienced superintendent turnover against comparable districts that did not, tracking outcomes in the years before and after the leadership transition. The multi-year window allows the authors to detect pre-existing trends as well as post-turnover recovery patterns.
For Practitioners: School boards should treat superintendent turnover as an academically costly event — not merely a management inconvenience. The evidence that instability precedes and partially explains the achievement decline means boards have an opportunity to intervene earlier: identifying and addressing district dysfunction before it forces a leadership change may matter more than optimizing the transition process after the fact. Boards in urban, large, or high-poverty districts face the steepest consequences and have the most to gain from proactive stability-building practices.

What the Research Asks and Why It Matters

Superintendent turnover is a persistent feature of American public education. Nationally, average superintendent tenure has hovered near three years for decades, and the churn is not evenly distributed — urban districts and those serving high concentrations of students in poverty experience leadership transitions at markedly higher rates than their suburban counterparts. Despite the frequency of these transitions, the research base directly linking superintendent turnover to measurable student outcomes has remained thin. Redding and Carlo set out to fill that gap using some of the most comprehensive longitudinal administrative datasets available in U.S. education research.

The authors draw on Florida and Texas not merely for convenience. Both states maintain detailed, longitudinal student- and district-level data systems, and both experienced substantial variation in superintendent tenure across the study period — providing the statistical leverage needed to isolate turnover effects. The combination of a rigorous quasi-experimental design and nearly a decade of data across two large and demographically diverse states produces estimates that are more credible and more generalizable than prior single-state or shorter-window studies.

The Achievement Effect: Small but Real

The headline finding — a −0.015 standard deviation decline in student achievement associated with superintendent turnover — requires careful interpretation. In isolation, that number sounds negligible. Researchers typically regard effect sizes below 0.05 standard deviations as educationally small. But the appropriate comparison is not to a single intervention effect; it is to the cumulative cost of repeated transitions over time. A district that cycles through three superintendents in nine years absorbs that loss three times over, compounding the drag on achievement trajectories in ways that the single-event figure does not capture. When the authors examine multi-year post-turnover windows, the picture that emerges is one of persistent disruption rather than a brief dip followed by recovery.

The event study design is particularly valuable here because it allows the authors to examine achievement trends in the years before a superintendent's departure, not just after. This pre-trend analysis reveals something important: in many cases, achievement was already declining before the superintendent left. Districts do not simply experience a sharp drop at the moment of transition — they often enter a turbulent period that precedes the departure and continues through the search, hiring, and onboarding process. The net effect is that the transition event is better understood as the most visible moment in a longer arc of institutional instability than as an isolated shock.

Who Bears the Largest Costs

The aggregate effect obscures sharp variation across district types. Urban districts, larger school systems, and those serving students in concentrated poverty show achievement declines after superintendent turnover that substantially exceed the overall average. This heterogeneity is not surprising, but it is consequential. These are precisely the districts that experience the highest turnover rates to begin with — creating a compounding disadvantage in which the communities most harmed by transitions are also the ones most likely to experience them repeatedly.

The authors' finding that the adverse outcomes are "partially explained by underlying district instability that precipitated the superintendent's departure" adds an important layer to the urban/poverty concentration pattern. Districts serving the most disadvantaged communities often face the most acute political and governance pressures — board conflict, community tension, fiscal stress — that precipitate leadership transitions. If those underlying conditions are not addressed, replacing the superintendent does not resolve the instability; it reshuffles it. This suggests that research and practitioner attention focused exclusively on superintendent selection and onboarding may be targeting the wrong leverage point. The conditions that make good superintendents want to stay — and that make their departure, when it comes, a managed transition rather than a crisis — are worth more than any improvement to the hiring process alone.

The Instability Mechanism

One of the study's most practically significant contributions is its attention to the mechanism linking turnover to outcomes. The authors find that pre-existing district instability accounts for a meaningful portion of the post-turnover achievement decline. This finding reframes the policy problem. If superintendents who leave were already operating in districts with deteriorating conditions, then the causal story is not simply "turnover causes achievement loss" but rather "district dysfunction causes both turnover and achievement loss, and they reinforce each other." Boards that want to protect student outcomes need to address the dysfunction — board conflict, unclear governance expectations, policy volatility, unstable budget conditions — that make superintendent departure likely in the first place.

This is not an exculpation of turnover's costs. Even after accounting for pre-existing instability, the transition itself carries a residual negative effect. The search process, the interim period, the onboarding of a new leader, the disruption to strategic continuity — these impose real costs that the pre-existing conditions do not fully explain. But the instability mechanism does suggest a hierarchy of interventions: preventing the conditions that drive turnover is more valuable than managing turnover more gracefully once it arrives.

Implications for Governance Practice

School boards are the governance actors most directly positioned to influence both the frequency of superintendent turnover and the conditions that surround it. The Redding and Carlo findings point toward several governance practices that the research supports as protective against the dynamics documented here.

  • Treat governance instability as an academic risk: Board conflict, frequent policy reversals, and unclear board-superintendent boundaries are not just management problems — they are predictors of the district dysfunction that this research identifies as a driver of both turnover and achievement loss. Boards should assess their own functioning as rigorously as they assess district academic performance.
  • Prioritize superintendent retention in high-need contexts: Urban boards and those governing large or high-poverty districts face the steepest achievement costs from turnover and the highest baseline risk of experiencing it. These boards have the most to gain from sustained investment in the board-superintendent relationship — structured goal-setting, clear evaluation frameworks, and explicit communication protocols.
  • Distinguish between departure types: Not all superintendent transitions carry equal costs. A planned retirement with a long runway, a carefully managed succession, and stable district conditions is a different event than an abrupt forced departure in the context of board conflict. The evidence on pre-existing instability suggests that the latter category is where the academic harm concentrates. Boards should examine what type of transitions they are generating and why.
  • Plan for continuity, not just replacement: The years immediately following a superintendent's departure are the highest-risk period for achievement. Boards that invest in transition planning — interim leadership protocols, clear communication to principals and staff, and an explicit commitment to strategic continuity during the search — reduce the duration and depth of the disruption window.
  • Address dysfunction before it forces turnover: The instability mechanism identified in this study suggests that the most valuable intervention may occur well before the superintendent considers leaving. Boards willing to examine and reform their own governance practices — including seeking external evaluation of board functioning — can reduce the pressure that makes departure likely.
The Bottom Line

Superintendent turnover imposes measurable costs on student achievement, with the largest harms falling on the districts — urban, large, high-poverty — that are already most vulnerable. Much of the damage is traceable to the district instability that drove the departure, not merely to the transition itself. For school boards, the practical implication is clear: the most effective strategy for protecting student outcomes from leadership disruption is building the governance conditions that make stable, long-tenured superintendent leadership possible in the first place.
