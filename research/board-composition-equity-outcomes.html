Core Finding: Using close school board elections as a natural experiment, this study finds that increasing minority representation on California school boards produces cumulative academic achievement gains of roughly 0.1 standard deviations among minority students by the sixth year after the election. These gains coincide with measurable increases in non-white district principals and greater capital funding — and do not come at the expense of white students, whose performance also shows improvement.
Methodology: Regression discontinuity design leveraging close California school board elections, comparing districts where a minority candidate narrowly won or narrowly lost. This quasi-experimental approach approximates random assignment near the electoral threshold, enabling credible causal inference about the effects of minority board representation on student outcomes and district administration.
For Practitioners: The research provides credible causal evidence — not just correlation — that who sits on the board shapes what happens for students. Boards and community organizations seeking to improve outcomes for minority students have evidence that increasing minority representation is itself a meaningful lever, operating through changes in administrative leadership and resource allocation rather than zero-sum redistribution from white students.

Why This Study Is Different

Research on school board composition and student outcomes faces a persistent methodological problem: districts that elect more minority board members are likely to differ from other districts in ways that independently predict student achievement. Isolating the causal effect of representation from these confounding factors requires a research design that can approximate random assignment. Kogan, Lavertu, and Peskowitz solve this problem by exploiting the near-randomness of close elections. When a minority candidate wins or loses by a handful of votes, the outcome is plausibly determined by factors unrelated to district characteristics — giving researchers a natural experiment in which districts with and without minority board members are otherwise comparable.

This regression discontinuity design is among the most credible quasi-experimental methods available to education researchers. The authors' ability to deploy it here — using detailed California school board election records linked to student achievement data and district administrative records — represents a methodological advance over the largely observational literature that preceded it. The findings it produces carry substantially more causal weight than studies relying on cross-sectional correlation or panel regression alone.

What the Evidence Shows

The study's headline finding is a cumulative achievement gain of approximately 0.1 standard deviations among minority students by the sixth year after a minority candidate's election — a meaningful effect that accumulates gradually rather than appearing immediately. This temporal pattern is itself informative: it suggests the mechanisms at work take time to develop, which is consistent with findings about how personnel and resource decisions ripple through school systems. Achievement does not jump the year a new board member is seated; it shifts as administrative leadership and funding priorities change.

Critically, these gains are not redistributed from white students. The study finds no evidence of negative effects on white student achievement — if anything, white students also show performance improvements. This finding challenges a zero-sum framing that sometimes shadows discussions of equity-focused governance. The evidence suggests that the policy changes driven by increased minority representation — more non-white principals, greater capital investment — improved conditions in ways that benefited students broadly.

On the mechanism side, the study finds two statistically significant pathways: a larger share of non-white district principals and greater capital funding in the years following a minority candidate's election. These are policy outputs squarely within a school board's governance authority — hiring and resource allocation decisions that do not require operational micromanagement. The study finds no significant effects on school segregation, English language learner reclassification, or teacher staffing, suggesting the equity impact flows primarily through administrative leadership representation and facilities investment rather than instructional workforce composition.

The Principal Pipeline as a Governance Lever

The finding that minority board representation increases the proportion of non-white district principals is particularly significant from a governance standpoint. School boards do not directly hire principals — that is a superintendent function — but boards set the direction and culture that shapes who superintendents recruit, support, and advance. A board whose composition reflects the district's student population creates different organizational signals about whose leadership is valued, which can cascade into hiring and promotion decisions across years and superintendent transitions.

Research consistently finds that students of color benefit academically and developmentally from having same-race school leaders. If minority board representation increases the non-white principal pipeline, it introduces a second-order equity mechanism that operates through representation at multiple levels of the system simultaneously. The board seat becomes a governance leverage point not just for policy decisions but for the organizational culture that shapes administrative pipeline development over time.

Capital Funding and the Equity of School Conditions

The study's finding on capital funding is a reminder that school board governance includes resource allocation decisions that shape the physical and material conditions of learning. Facilities inequality — the concentration of aging, underinvested school buildings in communities of color — is among the most visible and well-documented dimensions of educational inequity. The evidence that minority board representation is associated with greater capital investment suggests that who governs affects not just the policies written but the dollars directed, with downstream implications for the quality of the environment in which minority students learn.

This mechanism is especially important for practitioners to understand because capital funding decisions are among the clearest governance prerogatives — boards approve bond measures, set capital improvement priorities, and allocate facilities dollars through the budget process. The research suggests that when minority communities gain board representation, these high-stakes allocation decisions shift in ways that benefit minority students materially. That is governance working as democratic theory predicts it should.

Implications for Governance Practice

The findings from this study have direct implications for how boards, community organizations, and state policy actors think about the consequences of board composition. They are not merely symbolic. A minority candidate who narrowly wins a school board race produces measurable, six-year downstream effects on minority student achievement through identifiable policy mechanisms. The question "does representation matter?" now has a credibly causal answer: yes, and here is how.

  • Board recruitment matters for outcomes: Efforts to recruit and support minority board candidates are not only about democratic fairness — they are governance investments with documented student achievement consequences.
  • Administrative diversity follows board diversity: Boards seeking to develop a more representative principal pipeline should examine whether their own composition creates the organizational conditions that make diverse administrator hiring more likely.
  • Capital allocation is an equity tool: Boards committed to equity should examine whether capital planning processes systematically disadvantage schools serving minority students, and whether representation in the governance body correlates with more equitable capital investment.
  • Gains are not zero-sum: The evidence provides a direct rebuttal to arguments that equity-focused governance comes at the expense of non-minority students — in this study, white student achievement also improved following minority candidate elections.
The Bottom Line

Using a rigorous quasi-experimental design, this study provides credible causal evidence that electing minority board members in California improved minority student achievement by roughly 0.1 standard deviations over six years, operating through increases in non-white principals and capital funding. For practitioners, the research establishes that board composition is not merely a symbolic equity concern — it is a functional governance variable with documented consequences for the students most in need of effective representation.
