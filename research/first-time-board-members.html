Core Finding: School board members across Ohio districts identified a core institutional strength — their boards serve as sites of communal stewardship over the shared project of raising and educating children — but described that mission as increasingly threatened by structural barriers to effective governance and by the political polarization that intensified dramatically over the 2019–2023 term. Members understood themselves as democratic actors, yet found the institutions they served ill-equipped to protect democratic functioning under sustained cultural and partisan pressure.
Methodology: Descriptive-interpretive qualitative study drawing on interview data with school board members across Ohio districts who served during the 2019–2023 term. The interpretive framework situates school boards as democratic institutions, examining how member perspectives illuminate both the conditions that enable democratic governance and those that undermine it. The study does not seek causal claims or statistical generalization; findings represent patterns of meaning across participants' lived experience.
For Practitioners: Board members navigating polarized environments should understand that what they experience as personal stress or individual failure is often a structural problem — governance systems designed for an earlier political era that have not been adapted to contemporary conflict. Effective boards in this study found ways to anchor their work in the shared language of communal stewardship even when ideological division threatened that shared ground.

The Democratic Premise of Local School Governance

Knight-Abowitz approaches school boards not merely as administrative bodies that manage districts, but as democratic institutions — elected assemblies charged with translating community values into educational policy. This framing carries significant weight. It means that when school boards function poorly, the damage is not only operational (budgets mismanaged, superintendent relationships destabilized) but democratic: a local site of self-governance fails to hold. The 2019–2023 term tested this institutional premise more severely than any recent period in American education, and this study documents how board members across Ohio understood and navigated that test.

The study's descriptive-interpretive methodology is well suited to questions about meaning and institutional experience. Participants were not asked to report data or outcomes; they were asked to reflect on what it was like to serve, what felt possible, what felt foreclosed, and how they made sense of their role in a moment of acute political stress. The resulting portrait is neither triumphalist nor despairing — it is an honest account of democratic governance under strain, told by the people doing the work.

The Institutional Strength: Communal Stewardship

The most consistent theme across participants was an affirmative account of what school boards are for: the communal stewardship of shared resources and values in raising and educating healthy children. This framing is notable for what it includes and excludes. It positions the school board as a collective custodian — not of a single community faction's preferences, but of something shared across difference. Children are named explicitly as the beneficiaries, which grounds the institution in a purpose that cuts across partisan lines. And the word "stewardship" implies accountability to something larger than the present board — an inheritance from prior generations and an obligation to future ones.

Board members who oriented around this understanding of their role described a kind of governance that could survive disagreement. When debates over curriculum, budget, or personnel were framed as questions about what serves children and what the community can sustain together, participants found that meaningful deliberation remained possible even amid political pressure. The institutional strength, in Knight-Abowitz's analysis, is not procedural (meeting structures, parliamentary rules) but moral: a shared purpose capacious enough to hold diverse people together in common work.

The Institutional Vulnerabilities

Against that strength, the study documents two categories of vulnerability. The first is structural: persistent features of school board governance that create barriers to effective service regardless of political climate. These include inadequate preparation for the complexity of the role, insufficient support for navigating legal and policy terrain, and governance designs that concentrate conflict without providing institutional resources for managing it. Many participants described feeling under-equipped for what the job actually required — not because they lacked commitment or intelligence, but because the institution itself does not reliably develop the capacity its members need.

The second vulnerability is contextual but now arguably durable: the intensification of political polarization that reshaped the school board landscape between 2019 and 2023. This period saw school boards become focal points for national political movements — over masking and vaccination policies during the COVID-19 pandemic, over curriculum content related to race and gender, and over the broader question of what public schools are for and who controls them. Participants described this as a qualitative shift, not merely an increase in conflict intensity. Governance norms that had previously moderated disagreement — shared premises about public education's purpose, informal expectations of civility, the assumption that elections settled questions until the next election — were challenged in ways that structural procedures were not designed to address.

What Political Polarization Does to Democratic Institutions

Knight-Abowitz's analysis resists treating polarization as simply an external force that disrupts otherwise healthy institutions. The vulnerability she documents is partly internal: school boards as democratic institutions lack robust mechanisms for protecting democratic functioning when the political environment itself becomes anti-democratic in character. When board seats are contested as vehicles for ideological capture rather than community governance, when public meetings become theaters for national political performance rather than local deliberation, and when members face harassment and intimidation as routine features of service, the institutional norms that sustain democratic governance erode from within.

This has direct implications for how we understand the recruitment and retention crisis affecting school boards nationally. If service increasingly exposes members to personal and professional risk without providing institutional protection or support, the pool of willing candidates narrows — and narrows predictably. People with the most to lose (working parents, members of minority communities, those with professional reputations that can be targeted) face the highest exit pressure. The democratic representativeness of school boards, already imperfect, is further strained.

Implications for Governance Practice

This study does not offer a governance reform agenda, but its findings suggest several directions for practitioners and policymakers who care about school boards as democratic institutions. The resilience that participants described was almost always relational and normative — grounded in shared commitment to children and community, maintained through deliberate relationship-building across difference — rather than procedural. Structural reforms matter, but they are insufficient if the moral architecture of the institution is not also attended to.

  • Invest in board member preparation: Structural barriers to effective service are partly an inadequate-preparation problem. New member onboarding that goes beyond legal compliance and addresses the relational, political, and ethical dimensions of the role would reduce the vulnerability Knight-Abowitz documents.
  • Anchor board culture in communal purpose: Boards that explicitly and regularly articulate their shared purpose — the communal stewardship of children's education — build the normative foundation that resists capture by narrower political agendas. This is not political neutrality; it is a robust positive account of what the institution is for.
  • Protect the conditions for deliberation: When public meetings are structured in ways that maximize heat rather than enable deliberation — or when board members face insufficient institutional protection from harassment — democratic governance suffers. Boards, state associations, and legislatures all have roles in strengthening these conditions.
  • Take member retention seriously as a democratic concern: The decision of capable people not to run for or to leave school board seats is not just an HR problem; it is a democratic problem. Understanding why service has become more costly and addressing those costs is part of protecting local democratic governance.
The Bottom Line

School boards possess a genuine institutional strength — the communal stewardship of a shared commitment to children — but this strength is fragile and requires active maintenance. The 2019–2023 term revealed how quickly structural underinvestment and political polarization can erode the conditions that make democratic local governance possible. Practitioners who want their boards to function well in the current environment must do more than manage conflict: they must deliberately rebuild the institutional culture and structural supports that allow diverse people to govern together.
