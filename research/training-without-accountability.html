Core Finding: States that mandate professional development for school board members consistently scored higher on Education Week's 2009 "Quality Counts" ratings than states that leave training voluntary. Most states do not require training, yet state school board association directors widely believe such training positively affects student outcomes — a gap between belief and policy that this study documents directly.
Methodology: Survey of all 50 state school board association directors, with 26 responding. Responses captured director perceptions of training impact and documented each state's professional development mandate status. Researchers then compared that mandate status against Education Week's independent 2009 state education system ratings, creating a policy-comparison framework that links governance accountability requirements to an external quality benchmark.
For Practitioners: Board members and state policymakers should treat professional development as a governance accountability floor, not a voluntary benefit. Training as Optional: The Default That Fails Governance

School board members govern some of the most consequential public institutions in American civic life. They hire and evaluate superintendents, adopt district policy, set budgets, and make decisions that directly shape the conditions for student learning — often with no prior preparation required for any of it. Roberts and Sampson (2011) document the result of this voluntary default: as of 2009, most states in the United States did not mandate professional development for school board members. The implicit assumption is that election to office is sufficient qualification for governance. The research on governance quality suggests otherwise.

When training is optional, participation skews toward those who already value learning — often the board members who need it least. The boards facing the most acute governance challenges, the ones most likely to generate superintendent turnover, legal disputes, or policy incoherence, are frequently the same boards where norms of continuous improvement are weakest. A voluntary system cannot systematically close that gap. Roberts and Sampson's survey found that state school board association directors broadly believed training improved outcomes, but belief without a mandate produces uneven participation at best.

What the Education Week Comparison Shows

The study's most concrete finding is a policy comparison: states with professional development mandates for board members clustered in Education Week's B and C rating bands, while states without such mandates clustered in the C and D range. Education Week's annual "Quality Counts" assessment evaluates state education systems across multiple dimensions — standards and assessments, accountability structures, school finance equity, and student achievement data — providing an independent external benchmark that the study could map against governance policy choices.

This is a correlational pattern, not a controlled experiment. States differ on dozens of variables that could explain both the presence of training mandates and higher education ratings — fiscal capacity, political culture, population characteristics, longstanding policy traditions. The study makes no claim to have isolated training's independent contribution. What the pattern plausibly reflects is that states willing to impose a governance accountability requirement for board members are also states that invest more broadly in education system infrastructure. The mandate functions as both a direct intervention and a signal: governance seriousness at the board level tends to co-occur with governance seriousness across the system.

Why State Association Directors Believe Training Matters

The survey's director responses document the practitioner theory of change behind training advocacy. Directors — who observe governance quality variation across their states, interact with board members at all experience levels, and often deliver training directly — reported strong agreement that professional development positively affects student outcomes. Their implicit reasoning: board members who understand district finance, curriculum policy, superintendent evaluation, and governance roles make better decisions. Poor decisions by undertrained boards consume administrative attention, generate legal and political disruption, distract superintendents from instructional leadership, and signal to communities that accountability does not apply to the governing body.

This theory of change is consistent with the broader school board governance literature, which links board practices — goal clarity, coherent policy, effective superintendent evaluation — to district performance. Roberts and Sampson are appropriately cautious: the director survey captures perception, not demonstrated causal pathways. The study does not trace specific training content to specific board behaviors to specific student outcomes. What it does establish is that the professionals closest to state-level governance quality believe the connection is real, and that the absence of a mandate represents a structural accountability failure rather than a benign policy choice.

Limitations and What the Study Cannot Establish

The Roberts and Sampson design has real evidentiary constraints that practitioners should weigh carefully. The 26-of-50 response rate means the state comparison is based on a partial sample; the states that did not respond may differ systematically from those that did. More fundamentally, the Education Week comparison is uncontrolled: without holding constant confounding state characteristics, the link between training mandates and state ratings is suggestive but not definitive. The study also does not examine what training mandates actually require — how many hours, which content areas, whether completion is verified or enforced. A mandate on paper with no accountability mechanism may produce little behavioral change, meaning the relevant variable might be mandate quality rather than mandate existence.

The 2009 data also reflects a governance landscape that has shifted considerably. State training mandate policies, the content of board professional development, and the political context for governance reform have all evolved since the study was published. The directional argument — that mandatory training is associated with better outcomes than voluntary training — remains plausible, but the specific state comparisons warrant updated analysis.

Implications for Governance Practice

The study's central implication is structural: voluntary professional development will not close the governance preparation gap that exists across American school boards. The recommendation that all states require board member training across all domains of public schooling is direct and consistent with the evidence Roberts and Sampson assemble. Policymakers, state school board associations, and national governance reform organizations should treat this finding as a call for systemic change rather than individual encouragement.

  • Mandate, don't recommend: The gap between widespread director belief in training's value and the absence of state mandates is the study's clearest policy problem. Belief without policy produces voluntary participation — which systematically excludes the boards that most need it.
  • Accountability requires enforcement: A training mandate without completion verification replicates the accountability gap the training is meant to address. Mandates need enforcement mechanisms to produce consistent behavior change.
  • Breadth matters: Roberts and Sampson emphasize training across all areas of public schooling — not just procedural orientation. Board members need preparation in finance, curriculum, personnel policy, and governance roles to make quality decisions.
  • Read the Education Week pattern as a policy signal: Even absent controlled evidence, the consistent association between training mandates and higher state education ratings gives lower-rated states a governance reform hypothesis worth testing through updated, more rigorous analysis.
The Bottom Line

Most states do not require school board members to receive professional development — despite evidence that states with such requirements tend to have stronger overall education systems. The study documents a structural accountability gap: the officials making the highest-stakes decisions in public education are subject to less preparation accountability than most other licensed public roles. Treating board training as mandatory governance infrastructure rather than optional enrichment is a policy change with a plausible rationale and no identified downside.
