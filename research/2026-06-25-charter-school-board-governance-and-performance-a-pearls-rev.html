Charter school board governance and performance: A PEARLS Review

June 23, 2026
Study Under Review:
Ford, M. R., & Ihrke, D. M. (2020). Connecting group dynamics, governance, and performance: Evidence from charter school boards. *Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly, 49*(3), 546–567. https://doi.org/10.1177/0899764020911206

Study Under Review: Ford, M. R., & Ihrke, D. M. (2020). Connecting group dynamics, governance, and performance: Evidence from charter school boards. Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly, 49(3), 546–567. https://doi.org/10.1177/0899764020911206

This study investigates how board group dynamics translate into governance quality and ultimately organizational performance in charter schools. It merits PEARLS review because it addresses a persistent question in school governance research: whether and how board functioning matters for student outcomes. For a full explanation of the PEARLS framework used in this review, see our [methodology page](/methodology.html).

P — Peer Review

The study appears in Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly, a peer-reviewed journal, placing it among the more respected outlets in nonprofit governance scholarship. The journal employs standard double-blind review, providing reasonable confidence in its quality gatekeeping. However, the empirical approach—cross-sectional survey methodology linked to board performance data—does not face the same evidentiary thresholds as experimental or longitudinal designs. The venue suits research connecting board dynamics to organizational outcomes, though the specific application to charter schools occupies a narrower slice of the broader nonprofit governance literature.

E — Evidence Quality

The authors use a quantitative, cross-sectional design. They survey charter school board members and link those responses to board-level performance data, then apply multivariate regression analysis to test associations between governance characteristics and outcomes. The cross-sectional design is a significant limitation: it captures relationships at one point in time rather than tracking how governance dynamics unfold and produce outcomes over time. The authors acknowledge this and appropriately avoid causal language in their framing. Sample sizes appear adequate for the regression models employed, though the exact numbers require verification from the full text. Self-reported governance measures introduce potential common-method bias, as board members may present their boards favorably. The authors address this through multiple-item scales and statistical controls, but the limitation persists. Any claims about causality exceed what the design supports.

A — Applicability

The findings apply to charter school boards in the United States. Generalizing to traditional public school boards requires caution, as the authors explicitly note. A rural school board operating under elected governance, state-mandated policies, and collective bargaining agreements faces fundamentally different conditions than a charter board appointed by a nonprofit operator. The study's sample likely overrepresents certain geographic regions and charter sectors, so applicability varies by context. Practitioners should ask whether their board operates under similar governance structures, authorizer relationships, and market pressures before drawing direct lessons.

R — Replicability

No direct replication studies testing Ford and Ihrke's charter-board group dynamics model appear in the peer-reviewed literature. The closest analogues are conceptually related rather than true replications. Ford and Ihrke's own earlier work on traditional public school districts found limited or mixed evidence that governance best practices improve academic performance, which qualifies rather than contradicts the 2020 charter findings. Other nonprofit governance studies report positive but modest and context-dependent relationships between board functioning and organizational effectiveness. The absence of direct replication means the 2020 findings remain preliminary rather than established. Methodological transparency in the primary study—clear variable definitions and regression specifications—would help future replication attempts.

L — Limitations

The authors state four key limitations. First, charter school board data may not generalize to traditional public schools. Second, the cross-sectional design cannot establish causality. Third, self-reported governance measures may introduce bias. Fourth, unmeasured contextual factors may influence results.

Beyond these stated limitations, several additional concerns emerge. The performance measures likely capture a narrow band of outcomes, potentially missing important dimensions of school quality. The sample composition matters: if high-performing charter networks are overrepresented, the findings may reflect selection effects rather than governance effects. The study does not appear to control for authorizer quality or regulatory environment, both of which shape charter board autonomy and performance. The regression models assume linear relationships that may not capture complex interactions among governance variables.

S — Significance

This study extends the authors' earlier work on traditional public school boards into the charter sector, testing whether group dynamics matter for organizational performance in a different institutional context. The findings that trust, role clarity, and low conflict correlate with better outcomes align with broader nonprofit governance theory, though effect sizes remain modest. The study does not overturn prior knowledge but adds evidence from an understudied sector. For practitioners, it offers a research-based framework for thinking about board functioning, though the cross-sectional design limits confidence that manipulating governance variables will produce desired performance changes.

Practitioner Takeaways

Practitioners can reasonably conclude that board-level group dynamics, trust, role clarity, and low internal conflict, appear associated with better charter school performance. They should not conclude that adopting specific governance practices guarantees improved outcomes, as the cross-sectional design cannot establish causation. A school board should not assume the findings translate directly given different governance structures and accountabilities. Actionable steps include assessing role clarity among board members, monitoring conflict levels, and investing in board development that strengthens trust. However, practitioners should weight this study moderately given its limitations: the design is cross-sectional, the context is specific to charters, and no direct replication confirms the findings.
