School board composition and district outcomes: A PEARLS Review

June 23, 2026
Study Under Review:
Kogan, V., Lavertu, S., & Peskowitz, Z. (2023). Expertise and independence on governing boards: Evidence from school districts. Journal of Public Economics, 220, 104829. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpubeco.2023.104829

Study Under Review: Kogan, V., Lavertu, S., & Peskowitz, Z. (2023). Expertise and independence on governing boards: Evidence from school districts. Journal of Public Economics, 220, 104829. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpubeco.2023.104829

This study uses a clever research design to address a fundamental question for school board governance: does electing professional educators to school boards change how districts operate? The authors exploit California's randomized ballot order for school board candidates as an instrumental variable, allowing them to make causal claims about the effects of educator representation. This approach addresses a gap in board governance research, which often relies on correlational evidence. For a full explanation of the PEARLS framework used in this review, see our [methodology page](/methodology.html).

P — Peer Review

The study appears in the Journal of Public Economics, a top-tier peer-reviewed journal in the field of public economics. The journal maintains rigorous standards and is indexed in major academic databases, with a strong reputation for methodological rigor. The peer review process at this venue involves multiple rounds of evaluation by experts in public economics and political economy, which provides reasonable confidence in the study's technical execution. The authors are established researchers in education governance and political science, lending credibility to the analytical approach. There is nothing suspect about the publication venue or review process.

E — Evidence Quality

The methodology is a quasi-experimental design using California's randomized ballot order as an instrumental variable for whether professional educators win school board seats. This design addresses a central problem in board composition research: the non-random selection of board members, which confounds correlations between board characteristics and district outcomes. The sample includes a large number of California school districts across multiple election cycles, providing substantial statistical power. The design is longitudinal in the sense that it tracks outcomes over time following elections, which is appropriate for capturing effects on district inputs and student achievement.

The authors are careful to frame their findings as causal effects of electing educators at the margin, not as effects of overall board composition. This distinction is methodologically important and appropriately stated. However, readers should note that the instrumental variable approach estimates local average treatment effects for compliers (those who would have been affected by ballot order), which may not generalize to all districts or all educator candidates. The study does not directly observe the mechanisms linking educator election to outcomes; these are inferred from patterns in the data.

A — Applicability

The geographic scope is limited to California, which has distinct institutional features including teacher union strength, charter school regulations, and local control governance structures. These conditions may not hold in other states. The study's findings apply most directly to urban and suburban California districts with competitive school board elections where ballot order randomization is relevant.

A rural Minnesota school board member reading this study should be cautious about direct application. Minnesota has different teacher union dynamics, a different charter school landscape, and different electoral contexts. The study tells us that in California, electing educators changes certain district inputs, but it does not establish that the same patterns would emerge in Minnesota's governance environment. The authors explicitly note this limitation, which is appropriate.

R — Replicability

Based on the replication evidence available, there is no peer-reviewed study that directly replicates the specific causal findings of Kogan, Lavertu, and Peskowitz. The closest related work is Shi and Singleton (2023), published in the American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, which also uses randomized ballot order to examine effects of board composition on education outcomes. That study supports the broader premise that board composition influences district outcomes but does not test the specific mechanisms around professional educator representation, teacher salaries, and charter enrollment that this study examines. No clear contradictions from comparable causal designs have been identified. The current evidence base should be viewed as emerging and not yet systematically replicated.

L — Limitations

The authors state four limitations. First, academic outcomes are limited to standardized test scores, which capture only a narrow dimension of educational quality. Second, findings are specific to California's institutional context and may not generalize. Third, causal mechanisms such as union bargaining are inferred from observed patterns rather than directly observed. Fourth, there are potential measurement issues in identifying educators from candidate occupational data.

Beyond these stated limitations, the study's focus on test scores as the sole student outcome measure is a significant gap. Test scores do not capture student well-being, college readiness, social-emotional development, or other outcomes that boards care about. Additionally, the study examines effects on district inputs (teacher salaries, charter enrollment) but does not fully trace how these input changes translate to student experiences. The instrumental variable approach, while strong for establishing causality, identifies effects only for districts where ballot order was decisive, which may not represent typical board decision-making.

S — Significance

This study makes a meaningful contribution by providing causal evidence on a question that has largely been addressed with correlational data. The finding that educator-elected boards increase teacher salaries and reduce charter enrollment while leaving test scores unchanged is itself significant and somewhat counterintuitive. It suggests that board expertise influences district resource allocation but may not directly improve measured student outcomes. The study extends prior work on school board composition by moving beyond representation to examine professional expertise, and it challenges simplistic assumptions that board expertise automatically improves student achievement. However, the magnitude of the contribution should be calibrated against the limited replication evidence and the California-specific context.

Practitioner Takeaways

Practitioners can reasonably conclude that electing professional educators to school boards may shift district priorities toward teacher compensation and away from charter school expansion, at least in contexts similar to California. They should not conclude that educator representation definitively improves student outcomes, as the study finds no detectable effects on test scores. School board members considering this research should recognize that the study addresses board composition effects, not overall governance quality, and that California-specific conditions may limit generalizability.

Actionable takeaways: First, boards should not expect that simply electing educators will raise test scores. Second, teacher salary negotiations and charter school decisions may be influenced by board composition. Third, the mechanisms behind these effects deserve further investigation before policy prescriptions are made. Given the evidence quality and limitations, practitioners should give this study moderate weight in governance decisions, recognizing that it provides valuable causal evidence within a specific context but does not establish universal principles for board effectiveness.
