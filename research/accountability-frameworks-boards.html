Core Finding: School boards in higher-achieving districts score measurably higher on a validated instrument measuring board leadership practices. Factor analysis of the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey (EBLPS) identified six discrete dimensions of board behavior associated with student achievement, and a t-test confirmed that high-achieving districts scored significantly higher than demographically comparable low-achieving districts on the composite measure.
Methodology: The study developed and validated the 33-item EBLPS through a three-stage process: content validity (established via literature review of 12 board leadership practices), construct validity (established via exploratory factor analysis yielding six factors), and predictive validity (established via independent-samples t-test comparing mean EBLPS scores between high- and low-achieving school districts).
For Practitioners: Boards can use the EBLPS framework as a structured self-assessment tool against the six leadership dimensions it identifies. The instrument's validation means it captures something real about governance behavior — but practitioners should treat self-reported scores as diagnostic inputs, not performance proof, and pair self-assessment with external observation and outcome data.

What This Study Set Out to Do

School board governance research has long been criticized for relying on theoretical frameworks and case-study evidence without measurement instruments capable of distinguishing effective from ineffective board behavior in systematic ways. Johnson's 2013 study addressed this gap directly by developing and validating the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey — a 33-item instrument grounded in a literature review of 12 board leadership practices previously identified as supportive of student achievement. The study's aim was not to describe effective boards qualitatively but to build a measurement tool that could be applied at scale and tested against actual outcome data.

This is a technically important contribution. Much governance research operates at the level of case studies, matched-pair comparisons, or theoretical synthesis. An instrument with demonstrated content, construct, and predictive validity provides practitioners and researchers with something more operationally useful: a structured way to assess board behavior before outcome data arrives and a defensible basis for inferring that higher-scoring boards are doing something genuinely associated with better district results.

The Six Factors of Effective Board Leadership

Factor analysis of the 33 EBLPS items revealed six underlying dimensions that capture the structure of effective board leadership practice. While the specific factor labels and loadings require access to the full study, the factor structure effectively tells us that the 12 board leadership practices identified in the literature are not independently weighted — they cluster into coherent behavioral patterns. This clustering matters because it suggests that board effectiveness is not a checklist of discrete habits but a smaller set of underlying orientations that generate multiple observable behaviors.

The reduction from 12 practices to six factors also has practical implications for board development. Rather than attempting to address 12 separate competency areas, boards and governance coaches can focus development efforts on the six underlying factor orientations. Improvement in a factor orientation is likely to produce correlated improvement across the practices that load onto it, making development work more efficient.

Predictive Validity: The T-Test Finding

The study's most consequential result is its predictive validity test. After establishing that the EBLPS measures something real and coherent, Johnson tested whether it measured something that matters — specifically, whether boards in high-achieving districts scored differently on the instrument than boards in low-achieving districts. The t-test showed a statistically significant difference in mean EBLPS scores between the two groups. High-achieving districts had measurably higher board leadership practice scores.

This finding is correlational, not causal, and the study does not claim otherwise. Higher board scores do not prove that better governance caused higher achievement — selection effects, district resource differences, and community factors could all contribute to both. But the correlation is meaningful in a specific way: it establishes that the instrument is not measuring noise. What the EBLPS captures is systematically associated with student achievement outcomes, which gives practitioners a principled basis for using it as one input in governance improvement efforts.

Implications for Governance Practice

The EBLPS represents one of the more rigorous attempts in the governance literature to move from qualitative description of effective board behavior toward quantifiable assessment. For practitioners, the instrument offers several concrete uses: baseline self-assessment before a governance improvement initiative, pre/post comparison to track behavior change over time, and structured conversation with board members about which of the six factor dimensions most need development attention.

  • Use factor scores, not just totals: Composite EBLPS scores are useful for comparing across districts, but factor-level scores reveal which specific dimensions of board behavior are strongest and weakest — enabling targeted development rather than generic governance training.
  • Pair self-report with external observation: Survey instruments that ask board members to assess their own behavior are susceptible to social desirability bias. The most valid uses of the EBLPS combine board self-assessment with superintendent ratings, board observer ratings, or structured review of meeting transcripts and minutes.
  • Connect to outcome monitoring cycles: Boards that use the EBLPS periodically can track whether governance behavior changes coincide with outcome trajectory changes — creating a feedback loop between governance improvement efforts and student results over time.
  • Treat the six factors as a governance architecture: The factor structure is as useful as the composite score. Boards that score well on mission-related factors but poorly on accountability-related factors have a different development need than the reverse. The architecture tells the story; the composite score summarizes it.
The Bottom Line

Johnson's study provides one of the more technically rigorous validations in the school board governance literature — a 33-item instrument grounded in prior research, internally coherent by factor analysis, and predictively associated with student achievement differences. Boards should treat the EBLPS as a well-designed diagnostic tool, not a performance guarantee: it identifies behavior patterns associated with better outcomes, gives practitioners a structured vocabulary for governance assessment, and provides a measurement foundation for tracking improvement over time.
