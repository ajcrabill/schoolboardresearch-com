Core Finding: Factor analysis of the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey (EBLPS) revealed six underlying dimensions of effective board governance. T-tests comparing high- and low-achieving school districts produced statistically significant score differences, establishing criterion validity for the instrument and empirical support for the claim that specific board leadership behaviors are associated with differential student achievement.
Methodology: Survey validation study. A literature review identified 12 board leadership practices with documented associations with student achievement, which were operationalized into a 33-item survey instrument. Exploratory factor analysis identified the underlying dimensional structure. Criterion validity was tested by comparing EBLPS scores between high-achieving and low-achieving districts using independent-samples t-tests.
For Practitioners: This study provides a validated, research-grounded instrument for assessing board governance quality across six discrete behavioral dimensions. Boards that want to move beyond compliance-oriented self-assessment toward substantive governance improvement can use this framework to identify which specific behaviors distinguish high-performing from low-performing governance contexts — and to target development accordingly.

Context and Significance of the Study

The dominant strain of school improvement research for decades treated board governance as background — something to acknowledge and control for rather than study directly. That framing has been progressively challenged by a body of empirical work linking specific board behaviors to district-level outcomes. By 2013, several studies had established meaningful associations between board leadership practices and student achievement, but the field lacked a reliable, validated measurement tool that could be used for systematic research or practical governance assessment.

Johnson's 2013 study in the Journal of School Leadership addresses this gap directly. Its central contribution is not a new finding about which governance practices matter — prior literature had already suggested several — but rather the construction and validation of a tool that can measure those practices reliably. Instrument Development and Factor Structure

The EBLPS was built from a systematic review of the empirical literature on school board governance, from which 12 discrete board leadership practices with documented links to student achievement were identified. These 12 practices were operationalized into 33 survey items — a reasonable expansion that allows each construct to be measured with multiple indicators, which is standard practice in survey instrument development and improves reliability estimates.

Exploratory factor analysis of the 33-item instrument revealed a six-factor structure. The six factors represent distinct but related clusters of board behavior, suggesting that governance quality is multidimensional rather than a single, undifferentiated construct. This matters practically: a board that performs strongly on some dimensions and weakly on others cannot be adequately described by a single overall governance score. The six-factor structure allows more granular diagnosis — identifying specific areas for targeted improvement rather than generating a generic governance rating.

Criterion Validity: High vs. Low Achievement Districts

Instrument development alone would be a limited contribution. The study's methodological value is strengthened by the criterion validity analysis: comparing EBLPS scores between districts with high student achievement and districts with low student achievement. Independent-samples t-tests found statistically significant score differences across the instrument, providing empirical evidence that what the survey measures is not merely theoretical or procedural — it is consequential for student outcomes.

This criterion validity evidence is the study's most practically significant finding. A board governance survey that distinguishes high-achieving from low-achieving districts is not just a research tool; it is a diagnostic instrument with direct utility for governance improvement. The finding that the behaviors measured by the EBLPS differ meaningfully between high- and low-performing governance contexts establishes that these are the right things to measure — not just theoretically justified, but empirically grounded.

Implications for Governance Practice

The six-factor structure of the EBLPS offers boards and governance researchers a more precise vocabulary for discussing and assessing governance quality. Most board self-assessment instruments in common use measure procedural compliance — whether policies exist, whether evaluations occur on schedule, whether meetings follow parliamentary procedure. The EBLPS, grounded in the empirical literature on governance and student outcomes, operates at a different level: it measures the behavioral practices that research associates with consequential governance rather than compliant governance.

  • Multidimensional assessment: Boards can identify which of the six governance factors represent relative strengths and which represent development priorities, enabling targeted rather than generic improvement planning.
  • Research grounding: The 12 source practices were drawn from the empirical literature, not from practitioner preference surveys or normative governance frameworks — providing a more defensible basis for assessment than instruments derived from best-practice lists.
  • Criterion validity as accountability evidence: The demonstration that EBLPS scores differ between high- and low-achieving districts establishes that the instrument measures something consequential — a standard most governance self-assessment tools have not met.
  • Replicability potential: A validated instrument with a documented factor structure creates the infrastructure for replication studies, longitudinal tracking, and comparison across governance contexts in ways that ad hoc self-assessments cannot support.

Implications for Accreditation and External Governance Review

The practical value of this study depends on how the EBLPS is used. As a self-assessment tool, it provides a more research-grounded alternative to compliance-oriented governance checklists. As a research instrument, it enables more rigorous and comparable measurement of board governance across districts and over time. The criterion validity evidence — demonstrating that instrument scores differ significantly between high- and low-achieving districts — is important for any practitioner or policymaker who wants to move governance quality assessment from procedural compliance toward substantive outcome orientation.

  • Use for diagnostic self-assessment: The six-factor structure provides a governance improvement map — not a pass/fail compliance checklist, but a dimensional profile that can drive prioritized development efforts.
  • Inform accreditation criteria: External review processes that want to assess governance quality at a behavioral level — not just structural compliance — can use the EBLPS factor structure to identify the constructs worth measuring.
  • Support research on governance-to-outcomes links: Researchers studying the relationship between board behavior and student performance now have a validated instrument that has already demonstrated criterion validity, rather than needing to construct measures from scratch.
The Bottom Line

Johnson's study delivers what the governance research field needed: a validated, multidimensional instrument that measures board leadership practices linked to student achievement and distinguishes high-performing from low-performing governance contexts. Its value is primarily methodological — it gives boards, researchers, and external reviewers a more credible tool for assessing governance quality than most alternatives in common use. The study's cross-sectional design limits causal interpretation, but the criterion validity evidence is sufficient to justify using the EBLPS as a serious governance assessment instrument rather than a proxy measure.
