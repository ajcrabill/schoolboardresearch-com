The Core Argument: Turnout Shapes Policy

The central claim of Anzia's research is deceptively simple: when fewer people vote, organized groups matter more. In low-turnout elections, a well-mobilized constituency — one that can reliably turn out its members even when the broader public is disengaged — exercises disproportionate influence over who wins and, consequently, over what winners do once in office. School board elections are an unusually clean test of this mechanism because the dominant organized constituency is identifiable (teacher unions), the policy output is measurable (salary raises), and the variance in election timing across districts is large enough to generate meaningful comparisons.

Prior to this study, the theoretical case for a turnout-policy link in local elections was well-established but largely correlational. Districts that hold off-cycle elections tend to pay higher teacher salaries — but that could reflect underlying differences in political culture, wealth, or labor market conditions rather than election timing per se. What Anzia needed, and what the 2006 Texas law provided, was an exogenous shock that forced some districts to change their timing while leaving others unchanged. That natural experiment allows causal inference in a way that simple cross-district comparisons cannot.

What the Texas Law Made Possible

In 2006, the Texas legislature passed a law consolidating school board elections for districts below a certain size onto national Election Day in November. The law affected roughly 20% of Texas school districts, creating a treatment group that was forced to switch election timing for reasons unrelated to local political conditions. Critically, the affected districts did not choose to switch — they were required to — which means the decision was orthogonal to local factors that might otherwise confound the analysis.

Anzia paired this natural experiment with a matching design that constructed a comparison group of districts as similar as possible to the forced-switchers on observable pre-treatment characteristics. She then estimated the effect of switching to on-cycle elections on teacher salary raises in the years following the change, using fixed effects regression to control for time-invariant district characteristics and common trends. The result was a credible estimate of what the election timing change — holding everything else constant — did to compensation outcomes.

The Salary Finding and What It Implies

Districts that were forced to move to on-cycle elections granted significantly lower salary raises to teachers in subsequent years than comparable districts that remained on off-cycle schedules. This finding runs counter to what one might naively expect: if elections become more democratic and representative, shouldn't teachers — a large and politically active constituency — benefit? The answer depends on who the marginal voter is in each regime.

In off-cycle elections, the marginal voter is disproportionately union-affiliated or union-adjacent. Teacher unions invest heavily in mobilization efforts precisely because their institutional strength is magnified in low-turnout environments. In on-cycle elections with far higher turnout, the electorate more closely approximates the general public — including taxpayers and parents who may prefer that salary expenditures compete with other district priorities. The salary reduction finding is consistent with a story in which teacher unions were extracting a "low-turnout premium" from boards elected in low-turnout conditions, and that premium shrank when the electorate expanded.

It is important to note what this finding does not show. It does not establish that teacher salary levels in off-cycle districts were above the social optimum, or that on-cycle districts are paying teachers too little. The study identifies a distortion relative to median voter preferences — but median voter preferences are themselves contestable as a normative baseline. The governance implication is about whose preferences are reflected in board decisions, not about the intrinsic correct level of teacher compensation.

Implications for Governance Practice

Anzia's research is unusually useful for governance practitioners because it establishes a causal mechanism rather than just a correlation. The mechanism — organized groups extract more policy concessions in low-turnout elections — applies broadly beyond teacher salary. Any organized constituency with strong mobilization capacity has structural advantages in off-cycle board elections: developers, administrators, charter operators, parent activist organizations, and political clubs all face a lower mobilization threshold when overall turnout is low. The question for any district is not whether election timing creates this distortion but which organized groups have taken advantage of it.

  • Salary and compensation decisions: The most direct implication. Boards elected in low-turnout cycles may be systematically more responsive to union preferences on compensation, benefits, and staffing ratios than a median-voter baseline would support. This does not mean union preferences are wrong — it means they may not accurately reflect the full community's priorities.
  • Collective bargaining posture: If the board owes its electoral survival to organized labor mobilization, its willingness to negotiate vigorously at the bargaining table is structurally compromised, regardless of individual members' intentions.
  • Curriculum and policy decisions: Any organized group — not just unions — that can mobilize a committed bloc in low-turnout conditions gains leverage on issues within its domain. This is a structural feature of off-cycle elections, not a partisan one.
  • Superintendents navigating interest group pressure: Understanding the electoral origins of board member obligations can help superintendents calibrate which board positions represent genuine community preferences versus interest group commitments — an important distinction for reform strategy.

Scope and Limitations

The Texas context introduces some limits on generalizability. Texas school districts operate under specific state labor law and political culture conditions that differ from other states, particularly those with stronger collective bargaining frameworks. The salary finding may be larger or smaller in other contexts depending on how organized and well-resourced local teacher associations are. Additionally, the study focuses on a specific, easily measurable policy output (salary raises); the election timing effect on less quantifiable governance decisions — board culture, superintendent evaluation rigor, strategic planning ambition — remains understudied.

The study's quasi-experimental design is a substantial strength but not a perfect substitute for randomization. The matching procedure relies on the assumption that observable pre-treatment characteristics fully capture the relevant differences between switchers and non-switchers. If there are unobserved differences — for instance, if the districts required to switch had political dynamics that were already moving in a different direction — the causal estimate could be biased. The author conducts robustness checks that are largely reassuring, but the caveat is standard for natural experiment designs and should be acknowledged.

The Bottom Line

This study provides the clearest causal evidence to date that election timing is not a neutral administrative choice — it is a governance lever that determines whose preferences boards are most responsive to. Off-cycle, low-turnout elections produce boards that deliver more favorable policy outcomes to organized constituencies. If a district's goal is governance that reflects the full community's preferences, election timing belongs in the reform conversation.
