School board composition and district outcomes: A PEARLS Review

June 23, 2026
Study Under Review:
Jain, P., & Fahlen, G. (2024). Expertise and independence on governing boards: Evidence from school districts. *AERA Open, 10*(1), 1–22. https://doi.org/10.1177/23328584241323899

Study Under Review: Jain, P., & Fahlen, G. (2024). Expertise and independence on governing boards: Evidence from school districts. AERA Open, 10(1).

This study uses California's randomized ballot order to isolate causal effects of educator representation on school boards, examining outcomes including teacher salaries, charter enrollment, and student test scores. The quasi-experimental design addresses a persistent challenge in governance research: separating the effects of board composition from confounding district characteristics. For a full explanation of the PEARLS framework used in this review, see our [methodology page](/methodology.html).

P — Peer Review

AERA Open is a peer-reviewed, open-access journal published by the American Educational Research Association. The journal has established itself as a venue for rigorous empirical work, and its open-access model does not diminish its methodological standards. The study's authors are affiliated with academic institutions and present their work through the standard peer-review process. This publication venue provides reasonable confidence that the manuscript underwent scrutiny from reviewers with expertise in education policy and quantitative methods. There is no indication of any concerns about the review process itself.

E — Evidence Quality

The methodology employed here is a quasi-experimental design that exploits California's randomized assignment of candidate ballot order in school board elections. This approach represents a genuine strength: ballot order randomization creates plausibly exogenous variation in whether professional educators gain board seats, allowing the authors to estimate causal effects rather than mere correlations. The sample includes a large number of California K-12 districts across multiple election cycles, which provides adequate statistical power.

The authors use regression models to compare districts where educator candidates benefit from ballot order advantages to otherwise similar districts. This design addresses selection concerns that typically plague board composition research, where educators who win seats may differ systematically from non-educator winners. However, the design has limits. The treatment—having at least one professional educator on the board—is relatively coarse, and the study cannot unpack how individual board members influence collective decisions. The authors appropriately avoid strong causal language in most places, though some framing occasionally drifts toward overstating what a single mechanism identification strategy can support.

A — Applicability

The geographic scope is explicitly limited to California, and this restriction matters substantially. California's school board election rules—including ballot order randomization—do not apply in most other states. The state's educational context also differs in important ways: its charter school sector is large, its teacher salary structures are distinct, and its district demographics vary widely. A rural Minnesota board or a Texas urban district operates under different legal and political environments.

The study applies specifically to districts where professional educators appear on the ballot and where ballot order randomization plausibly affects election probability. This narrows the applicable population considerably. The findings speak most directly to districts with similar electoral structures and with sufficient educator candidates to generate variation. Practitioners in states without ballot order randomization should be cautious about generalizing, as the identification strategy depends entirely on this specific feature of California law.

R — Replicability

The replication evidence reveals no direct replications of Jain and Fahlen's design. No peer-reviewed study was found that uses randomized ballot order to examine board member expertise and independence effects on district outcomes. However, several related studies provide conceptually corroborating evidence.

Prior research has found significant associations between school board governance characteristics and educational quality in various settings, supporting the general claim that board governance matters for outcomes. Other quasi-experimental work has shown that changes in school board composition, including greater representation of particular groups, can have causal effects on student achievement and district spending priorities, though these studies examine different dimensions of board composition than this study. Correlational analyses in the governance literature have linked board models to board functioning, and some work has connected higher voter turnout in school board elections to differences in student achievement.

None of this related work contradicts the core finding that board composition affects district outcomes. It provides complementary evidence on different dimensions of governance. The absence of direct replication means the specific estimates—particularly the magnitude of effects on teacher salaries and charter enrollment—should be treated as preliminary until additional evidence accumulates.

L — Limitations

The authors acknowledge several limitations. The study is limited to California with its specific election rules, restricting generalizability. Student outcomes are measured only through standardized test scores, which capture only a narrow band of educational achievement. The classification of "educator" status relies on occupation labels, which may misclassify some individuals. The study does not examine internal board dynamics, and the outcome scope is limited to teacher pay, charter enrollment, and test scores.

Beyond these stated limitations, additional concerns emerge. The treatment definition—having at least one professional educator on the board—is binary and may mask important variation from having multiple educators or from the specific roles educators play. The study cannot distinguish between effects driven by educators' expertise versus their independence from other interests, as the title's framing suggests these are separable constructs. The analysis focuses on whether educators serve on boards but does not examine how their presence shapes board deliberation, budget priorities, or policy decisions beyond the three measured outcomes. Finally, the sample excludes districts where no educator runs for office, potentially biasing estimates if educator candidacy itself is endogenous to district characteristics.

S — Significance

This study makes a meaningful contribution by applying a credible identification strategy to a question that has long suffered from endogeneity problems. The use of ballot order randomization to create exogenous variation in board composition is a genuine methodological advance over prior work that relied on correlational analysis. The findings that educator representation increases teacher salaries and reduces charter enrollment are substantively important, suggesting that board expertise influences resource allocation decisions.

However, the significance should not be overstated. The study confirms what many practitioners likely intuit—that board members bring their professional perspectives to governance—while providing more rigorous evidence for this claim. It extends prior work by isolating causal effects but does not fundamentally challenge existing understanding. The null finding on test scores is itself valuable, suggesting that board composition effects may operate through institutional channels rather than direct academic intervention. The study adds to a growing body of evidence that school board characteristics matter for district governance, but it is one piece in a larger research picture.

Practitioner Takeaways

Practitioners can reasonably conclude that having professional educators on school boards may influence district policy priorities, particularly around teacher compensation and charter school authorization. The study provides credible evidence that board composition has measurable effects on resource allocation.

Practitioners should not conclude that educator representation automatically improves student outcomes, as the study finds no significant effects on test scores. They should also not generalize the specific effect sizes to other contexts without considering California's unique regulatory environment.

Actionable takeaways: First, boards should recognize that member backgrounds likely shape policy preferences, whether or not formal expertise requirements exist. Second, the finding that educator representation correlates with higher teacher salaries suggests that board composition may influence labor negotiations. Third, the reduction in charter enrollment associated with educator representation indicates that professional backgrounds affect attitudes toward school choice. Fourth, the absence of test score effects should temper expectations that simply changing board composition will improve academic outcomes.

Given the evidence quality and limitations, practitioners should give this study moderate weight—sufficient to inform thinking about board composition but not sufficient to drive specific policy changes without considering local context.
