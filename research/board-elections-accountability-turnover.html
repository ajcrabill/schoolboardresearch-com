For Practitioners: The research challenges a foundational assumption of local democratic governance: that elections discipline performance. The Paradox: High Churn, Weak Accountability

School board elections are premised on a straightforward democratic theory: voters observe district performance, evaluate incumbents accordingly, and replace those who fail to deliver. That theory turns out to be largely wrong — not because the elections fail, but because the mechanism by which boards turn over has almost nothing to do with electoral accountability.

Kogan, Lavertu, and Peskowitz assembled the most comprehensive election dataset in school board research to date — covering 16 states and thousands of races — and documented a striking pattern. Roughly half of all races are uncontested. Among incumbents who actually run for reelection, more than 80% win. Yet in a typical election cycle, 53% of board seats change hands.

These facts seem to contradict each other until you understand the mechanism: most board turnover comes from incumbents who choose not to run again, not from incumbents who run and lose. The high apparent "turnover rate" is largely a function of voluntary retirement, not democratic accountability. Voters rarely get the chance to weigh in on incumbent performance because incumbents who anticipate a competitive race — or who have simply reached a natural stopping point — exit before the election. The seat changes, but not because the public demanded it.

What the Data Actually Shows

The analysis surfaces several patterns that complicate standard narratives about school board elections as accountability mechanisms:

  • Nearly half of all school board races have no opposing candidate — voters cannot register dissatisfaction even if they want to.
  • When incumbents do seek reelection, they win more than 80% of the time, suggesting that the electoral bar for accountability is extremely high in practice.
  • The 53% seat-change rate per election cycle — which might look like vigorous democratic renewal — is driven primarily by voluntary retirements and decisions not to seek additional terms.
  • Board turnover, whether driven by retirement or defeat, is only weakly related to student learning rates — suggesting that personnel change alone does not improve outcomes.
  • These dynamics hold consistently across urban and non-urban districts, across different student demographic profiles, and regardless of local media environment.

The finding that turnover is uncorrelated with student outcomes is particularly consequential. It suggests that the natural cycling of board membership — the 53% replacement that happens every election cycle — is not selecting for or against governance effectiveness in any meaningful way. New board members are not systematically better (or worse) than the members they replace.

Why This Matters More Than Turnover Rate

The authors frame their finding as a critique of the democratic theory underlying local educational governance: elections are supposed to create incentives for incumbents to perform, and to remove those who don't. But if incumbents who anticipate poor performance outcomes simply retire rather than face voters, the accountability signal never fires. The election becomes a ritual of seat-filling rather than a mechanism for performance management.

This is distinct from the question of whether school board elections are competitive enough — a critique that typically focuses on low turnout, gerrymandered districts, or organized-group advantages. Kogan, Lavertu, and Peskowitz are making a stronger argument: even the elections that do occur, even the races that are contested, do not appear to produce accountability in the form of outcome-sensitive turnover or post-turnover improvement.

For researchers, this raises serious questions about the welfare economics of decentralized educational governance. For practitioners, it raises a more immediate question: if elections don't discipline performance, what structures actually do?

Implications for Governance Practice

The research does not suggest that school board elections are without value — representation, community voice, and democratic legitimacy are not reducible to performance incentives. But it does suggest that boards and superintendents who wait for the electoral cycle to resolve governance problems are relying on a mechanism that doesn't work as advertised.

Several governance implications follow from the evidence:

  • Internal accountability structures matter more than electoral accountability. Superintendent evaluation practices, board self-assessment processes, and goal-monitoring routines are likely to be more effective levers for performance improvement than hoping elections will do the work.
  • High turnover is not a signal of health. Boards that experience rapid membership change — even through elections — should not interpret that change as renewal. The research suggests churn is noise, not signal, relative to student outcomes.
  • Succession planning is a governance responsibility. If most seat changes come from voluntary retirement rather than electoral defeat, then how boards manage the transition — including onboarding, institutional memory, and goal continuity — matters significantly for whether new members are effective.
  • Community engagement supplements what elections can't deliver. If electoral accountability is weaker than assumed, the mechanisms that keep boards connected to community interests — advisory committees, structured public comment, transparent goal-setting — carry a heavier burden than electoral theory suggests they should.
The Bottom Line

Board seats turn over frequently, but not because voters are holding anyone accountable for results. The electoral mechanism that justifies local democratic governance is not functioning as democratic theory predicts. This is not a reason to abandon local governance — but it is a reason to invest heavily in the accountability structures that elections are not providing.
