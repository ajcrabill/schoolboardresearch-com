Core Finding: A validated 33-item survey of school board leadership practices — the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey (EBLPS) — produced a stable six-factor structure through factor analysis, and high-achieving school districts scored significantly higher than low-achieving districts on the instrument. This provides empirical evidence that specific, measurable board governance behaviors are associated with student outcomes.
Methodology: Quantitative survey validation study. A literature review identified 12 board leadership practices with documented links to student achievement, which were operationalized into 33 items. Exploratory factor analysis established construct validity by identifying six underlying governance dimensions. An independent-samples t-test compared EBLPS mean scores between high- and low-achieving school districts to establish criterion (predictive) validity.
For Practitioners: Boards can move beyond checklist-style self-assessment toward a validated, research-grounded instrument that measures the governance behaviors most associated with student outcomes. The six-factor structure gives boards a diagnostic map — identifying which governance dimensions are relative strengths and which warrant targeted development — rather than a single global rating that obscures where improvement leverage lies.

Why Board Governance Measurement Has Been Difficult

For much of educational research history, school board governance was treated as a background variable — legally significant but empirically peripheral. The assumption was that instructional quality at the classroom and school level was where student outcomes were determined, and that board behavior was too distal and too variable to study systematically. Governance research, when it existed, tended to produce case studies of dysfunction or descriptive surveys of board composition and meeting frequency, neither of which addressed the fundamental question: which specific board behaviors, practiced consistently, are associated with better results for students?

Johnson's 2013 study addresses this measurement gap directly. Rather than treating governance quality as a latent, unobservable variable, the study constructs and validates a survey instrument capable of producing reliable, comparable scores across districts. This is not a minor contribution. Without valid measurement tools, governance improvement efforts rest on theory and advocacy rather than evidence. The EBLPS represents an attempt to put school board practice on the same empirical footing as other leadership variables that researchers and practitioners already treat as consequential.

From Literature to Instrument: Building the EBLPS

The instrument development process followed a rigorous sequence. A systematic review of the school board effectiveness literature identified 12 board leadership practices with documented empirical connections to student achievement. These practices spanned the core functions of effective governance — including goal-setting, monitoring student outcomes, supporting and evaluating the superintendent, maintaining a policy focus, and engaging productively with the community. The 12 practices were operationalized as 33 survey items, producing the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey.

Construct validity was established through exploratory factor analysis, which revealed six significant underlying factors in the EBLPS data. This finding is substantively important: it means governance quality is not a single, undifferentiated trait but a multidimensional construct composed of distinct behavioral clusters. Boards can be strong on some factors and weak on others. A global governance score can mask meaningful variation in the specific competencies that drive outcomes — variation that targeted professional development could address if it were visible. The six-factor solution makes that variation visible.

Criterion Validity: High Achievers Practice Differently

The study's most practically significant finding comes from its criterion validity analysis. Using an independent-samples t-test, the study compared mean EBLPS scores between school districts classified as high-achieving and those classified as low-achieving. The difference was statistically significant, indicating that high-achieving districts were characterized by meaningfully higher levels of the governance behaviors the survey measures.

This finding closes an important inferential gap. Demonstrating that a factor analysis produces a coherent structure establishes that the instrument is measuring something real — but it does not, by itself, establish that what is being measured matters. The criterion validity evidence does. When boards in districts with better student outcomes score systematically higher on a governance instrument than boards in lower-performing districts, that instrument is capturing something that is not merely theoretical. The gap between how high- and low-achieving district boards operate is observable and measurable — and, by implication, addressable.

What the Six Factors Suggest About Governance Priorities

While the study does not provide a definitive label for each of the six factors — the specific factor structure requires direct access to the instrument and analysis details — the overall pattern is consistent with governance frameworks developed by the Iowa Lighthouse Inquiry, the National School Boards Association's Key Work of School Boards, and the Effective School Boards Initiative. Across this body of work, the governance behaviors most consistently associated with student outcomes cluster around: maintaining clear, measurable goals tied to student achievement; monitoring progress against those goals with discipline and regularity; supporting and holding accountable the superintendent through structured evaluation; establishing and respecting the governance-management boundary; engaging the community in support of district mission; and committing to board members' own professional development. The EBLPS six-factor solution likely reflects some version of this clustering, and boards seeking to use the instrument meaningfully should consider which of these domains they are most and least mature in.

Implications for Governance Practice

The most direct implication of this study is that governance quality can be measured — and therefore managed. Boards that treat their own performance as inherently subjective or political are operating without the feedback loops that enable improvement. The EBLPS offers a validated alternative: a tool whose criterion validity connects board behavior to the outcomes those boards exist to produce.

  • Self-assessment rigor: Boards should use validated instruments — not internally designed checklists — for governance self-assessment. The EBLPS provides an empirically grounded option whose items are anchored in the effectiveness literature rather than local preferences.
  • Multidimensional diagnosis: Because governance quality is six-dimensional, boards should review factor-level scores rather than relying on aggregate ratings. A strong global score can obscure a critical weakness in one area — such as superintendent evaluation quality or goal-monitoring discipline — that undermines student outcomes.
  • Targeted professional development: The six-factor structure enables boards to match professional development investment to actual diagnostic findings. Generic board training that covers all topics equally misallocates attention; factor scores allow prioritization.
  • Accountability framing: The finding that high-achieving districts have significantly higher board governance scores reframes board quality as a student outcomes issue, not just a procedural one. Governance improvement is not self-improvement for its own sake — it is a lever for the students the board serves.
The Bottom Line

Johnson's 2013 study provides what much governance research lacks: a validated, multidimensional measurement tool that connects board practice to student achievement. The six-factor structure of the EBLPS gives boards a diagnostic framework rather than a single score, while the criterion validity evidence establishes that what the instrument measures is consequential. For boards serious about improvement, this study makes the case that governance quality is not a matter of opinion — it is a measurable variable with measurable stakes.
