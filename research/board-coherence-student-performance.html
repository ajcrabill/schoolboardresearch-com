Core Finding: Superintendent self-other agreement on accountability behaviors predicts district student achievement, with effect sizes rarely seen in governance research: the self-other rating category alone explained 22% of the variance in district mathematics scores and 19% in reading, after controlling for socioeconomic status. Critically, the direction of the effect matters — superintendents who overestimated their accountability behaviors led significantly lower-performing districts than those who agreed with subordinate ratings or underestimated them.
Methodology: Quantitative survey study employing hierarchical multiple regression and analyses of covariance (ANCOVA), with district socioeconomic status as a control variable. Superintendents and their subordinate central office administrators completed survey instruments rating superintendent accountability behaviors; respondents were then classified into self-other agreement categories (agreement, overestimation, underestimation) and compared on district-level student achievement outcomes.
For Practitioners: Superintendent overconfidence about one's own accountability practices appears to carry a measurable cost in student outcomes. Boards overseeing superintendent evaluation should consider structured 360-style feedback that surfaces discrepancies between superintendent self-assessment and central office perceptions — not as a gotcha mechanism, but because the gap itself appears to be a meaningful signal about district performance.

Study Under Review:
Hough, K. L. (2014). Internal accountability and district achievement: How superintendents affect student learning. Journal of School Leadership, 24(1), 1–30. https://doi.org/10.1177/105268461402400102

Hough's 2014 study examines a question at the intersection of leadership psychology and district governance: whether superintendent self-awareness — specifically, whether superintendents accurately perceive their own accountability behaviors relative to how subordinate administrators perceive those behaviors — predicts district student achievement. The answer, it turns out, is yes, and with effect sizes that demand attention. For a full explanation of the PEARLS framework used in this review, see our methodology page.

P — Peer Review

The study was published in the Journal of School Leadership, a peer-reviewed journal established in 1991 and focused on school and district leadership research. The journal is indexed in major academic databases and listed on Scimago, where it occupies the Q2 tier in educational administration — a solid if not top-tier venue. It is not among the field's highest-prestige publications (Educational Administration Quarterly and the American Educational Research Journal carry more weight in the research community), but it applies standard blind peer review and publishes empirical work that engages mainstream methodological expectations in the field.

There are no indicators that the journal is predatory or operates with compromised review standards. The 2014 publication date means the peer review process occurred roughly a decade ago — a modest relevance consideration but not a significant one for a study whose core question (whether superintendent self-awareness matters) has not been superseded by subsequent research. The funding source was not disclosed, which is a minor transparency concern but not uncommon in education leadership research of this period.

E — Evidence Quality

The study's methodology is appropriate and reasonably sophisticated for the research question. Hierarchical multiple regression with SES as a covariate is the right tool for establishing whether self-other agreement predicts achievement beyond what socioeconomic factors already explain. ANCOVA allows the study to compare achievement across self-other agreement categories while holding SES constant. Both are standard approaches for this type of inquiry.

Several limitations constrain the evidence quality, however. First, the design is cross-sectional — data were collected at a single point in time, meaning the study can identify association but cannot establish that superintendent self-awareness causes better district outcomes rather than the reverse. High-performing district cultures may produce more accurate self-assessment climates; the study's design cannot distinguish between these explanations. Second, the accountability behavior measures are survey-based self-reports and subordinate reports. Self-report instruments are susceptible to social desirability bias, particularly when superintendents are rating their own accountability behaviors — a construct with obvious professional valence. Third, the abstract does not disclose the study's sample size or geographic scope, which limits independent assessment of statistical power and generalizability. The effect sizes reported (22% and 19% variance explained) are large enough that the findings are unlikely to be artifacts of small-sample sampling error, but without knowing the N and sample frame, this cannot be confirmed.

The operationalization of "self-other agreement" into three categories (agreement, overestimation, underestimation) is analytically productive but introduces a derived-variable artifact: the category membership depends on the gap between two imperfect survey instruments, meaning measurement error in either direction affects classification. The study would benefit from sensitivity analyses or documentation of how the classification thresholds were established.

A — Applicability

The study's applicability depends on several contextual factors that the abstract does not fully specify. The geographic and demographic scope of the sample — which states, what distribution of district sizes, what urban/rural/suburban mix — would determine how broadly the findings travel. A finding derived from a sample heavily weighted toward large urban districts may not apply in the same way to small rural districts where the superintendent-central office relationship is structurally different and the "subordinate central office administrators" category may include very few individuals, creating a different social dynamic for accountability self-assessment.

That said, the theoretical mechanism the study tests — that self-other rating agreement in leadership is associated with effectiveness — is well-grounded in organizational psychology research that spans diverse institutional contexts (Atwater & Yammarino, 1997; Yammarino & Atwater, 1997). The school district is not a uniquely exotic organizational environment with respect to this mechanism. The finding that overestimation predicts lower performance has face validity for any governance context in which leaders must accurately diagnose their own effectiveness to improve it.

For practitioners, the finding is moderately actionable. Boards conducting superintendent evaluations can incorporate structured multi-rater feedback as a standard component of the evaluation process — not as a disciplinary tool but as a diagnostic one. The specific behavioral domain (accountability behaviors) is consequential for governance: these are behaviors, like setting clear expectations and monitoring progress against them, that directly connect to student outcomes. The study does not, however, specify which accountability behaviors drove the effect, limiting the precision of any intervention.

R — Replicability

The study's core finding draws on a broader self-other rating agreement literature in organizational leadership that has been replicated extensively outside of education. Atwater and Yammarino's foundational work on self-other agreement in leadership (1997) established that overestimators tend to be less effective leaders — a finding corroborated in dozens of organizational studies across business, military, and nonprofit sectors. Hough's contribution is to extend that established pattern to K–12 school district leadership with student achievement as the outcome variable, which is a meaningful extension but not a standalone finding in an otherwise empty research space.

Within the education leadership literature specifically, direct replications of this specific design and outcome are limited. The study appears to be one of relatively few that operationalize the self-other agreement construct in the context of superintendent-central office relationships and link it to district-level achievement data. The methodology is sufficiently described in the abstract to allow replication in principle, but access to the survey instrument and full methodology details would require the full text. Given the cross-sectional design, longitudinal replications would substantially strengthen the causal story the study's effect sizes seem to tell.

L — Limitations

The most significant limitation — one the study's design cannot remedy — is the cross-sectional architecture. The study finds that self-other agreement category is associated with district achievement, but the causal pathway cannot be resolved from a single time-point survey. Three explanations are consistent with the data: (1) superintendent self-awareness causes better leadership behaviors, which cause better outcomes; (2) high-performing district cultures generate accurate feedback environments and thus produce agreement between superintendent and subordinate ratings; or (3) some third variable (e.g., superintendent experience, district stability, board quality) causes both accurate self-assessment and better outcomes. The study's design cannot distinguish among these, and the abstract's language — "superintendents who agreed... led districts with significantly higher student achievement" — implies a directional claim the design does not support.

The self-report measurement of accountability behaviors is a second structural limitation. Social desirability pressure is particularly strong for the construct in question: a superintendent describing their own accountability behaviors on a survey instrument administered by a researcher is describing, in effect, how much they hold themselves and others accountable. Overestimation may reflect genuine self-delusion, or it may reflect a respondent who is being diplomatic on a survey while subordinates are being frank. The study cannot distinguish between these psychologically distinct phenomena.

The funding source is not disclosed, which is a transparency gap. While there is no specific reason to suspect conflict of interest, non-disclosure in a study that measures accountability practices — a construct with professional development industry implications — is worth noting. The abstract also does not report key methodological details including sample size, response rate, geographic scope, and the method by which self-other agreement categories were defined, all of which limit external evaluation.

S — Significance

The study's significance is genuine and proportionate to its evidence base. A finding that explains 22% of variance in district mathematics scores and 19% in reading — after controlling for SES — is a large effect in governance research, where effect sizes of this magnitude are uncommon. Even allowing for the design limitations outlined above, an association of this strength warrants serious attention. The finding is also theoretically coherent: it connects a superintendent behavioral construct (accountability) to a well-established leadership psychology mechanism (self-other agreement) to a directly student-relevant outcome (achievement). That chain has practical implications at every link.

The study extends an underutilized framework — self-other rating agreement — into the K–12 governance domain, where superintendent evaluation has historically relied on board impressions and annual conversations rather than structured multi-rater feedback. The finding that overestimation is specifically the problem condition, not merely disagreement in either direction, is a precise and actionable insight. Underestimation did not carry the same negative association, suggesting that the issue is not mismatch per se but a specific pattern of inflated self-perception.

The significance holds even given the limitations: the cross-sectional design limits causal inference, but the strength of association justifies treating the self-other agreement construct as a meaningful diagnostic variable worthy of board attention and further longitudinal study.

Practitioner Takeaways

Practitioners can reasonably conclude that superintendent self-awareness about accountability behaviors is associated with district student outcomes in a manner that is both statistically and practically significant. The specific pattern — that overestimation, not merely disagreement, correlates with lower achievement — is a finding with direct implications for how boards structure superintendent evaluation and feedback.

Practitioners should not conclude that implementing 360-degree superintendent feedback will cause improved student outcomes. The cross-sectional design cannot support a causal claim, and the mechanism driving the association remains unresolved. The study supports investing in better diagnostic tools; it does not guarantee a specific return on that investment.

  • Structure superintendent evaluation to surface self-other gaps: Boards that rely exclusively on their own observations and superintendent self-reports are missing a diagnostic layer that this study suggests matters. Central office administrator perspectives on superintendent accountability behaviors are worth collecting systematically.
  • Treat overestimation as a yellow flag, not a character indictment: The study identifies overestimation of accountability behaviors as a pattern correlated with lower performance. Boards should approach this finding as diagnostic information — an indicator that calibration work may be needed — not as evidence of bad faith or incompetence.
  • Focus evaluation on accountability behaviors specifically: The construct the study measured is accountability behavior — setting expectations, monitoring, following through. These are the behaviors most directly connected to the governance-management interface. Board evaluation processes that focus heavily on relational or communication dimensions while underweighting accountability rigor may be missing the behaviors this research flags as consequential.
  • Weight this study's evidence appropriately: The effect sizes are large and the construct validity is theoretically sound. But the cross-sectional design, undisclosed sample details, and reliance on self-report measures mean this study is a strong prompt for further investigation and cautious practice change, not a mandate for immediate structural overhaul of superintendent evaluation systems.
The Bottom Line

Hough's 2014 study identifies a striking association between superintendent self-awareness and district student outcomes — one with effect sizes large enough to take seriously even in the presence of design limitations. The finding that overestimation of one's own accountability behaviors predicts lower district achievement provides a specific, actionable signal for boards conducting superintendent evaluation. The study cannot prove causation, but it provides a well-grounded empirical basis for incorporating multi-rater feedback into superintendent development and evaluation as a standard governance practice.
