Core Finding: States requiring professional development for school board members consistently received B or C ratings from Education Week's 2009 Quality Counts report, while states without such requirements tended to receive C or D ratings. Despite this pattern, most states do not mandate professional development for board members, and state school board association directors widely believe that such training positively affects student achievement.
Methodology: Researchers surveyed 26 state school board association directors to gather information about professional development requirements and perceptions. Survey responses were then compared against Education Week's 2009 Quality Counts state education ratings, creating a cross-sectional comparative analysis linking state-level board development policies to independently assessed education quality scores.
For Practitioners: Board members and policymakers should treat professional development not as an optional enhancement but as a governance infrastructure investment. The association between mandated board training and stronger state education ratings suggests that formalized learning expectations for board members — not just for teachers and administrators — belong in any serious governance improvement agenda.

The Core Question This Research Addresses

Does it matter whether school board members receive professional development? The intuitive answer is yes — board members govern billion-dollar organizations affecting the education of thousands of children, yet in most states they are elected with no formal training requirement whatsoever. Roberts and Sampson took that intuition and tested it empirically, comparing states that mandate board professional development against those that do not and examining whether the difference shows up in external assessments of education quality.

The study's approach is straightforward but instructive. Rather than attempting to trace a direct line from board training to test scores — a causal chain too long and too mediated to measure cleanly — the researchers used Education Week's Quality Counts ratings as a proxy for state-level education system quality. These ratings aggregate multiple indicators of education performance and policy. The question becomes not "does board training raise test scores?" but the more tractable "are states with board training requirements rated as better-performing education systems?" The answer, in this data, is yes.

What the Directors Said — and What the Data Showed

The survey of 26 state school board association directors revealed a striking gap between belief and policy. Directors broadly believed that professional development positively affects student achievement — a near-consensus view among the professionals who work most closely with boards across their states. Yet when the researchers examined actual state policies, they found that most states do not require professional development for school board members at all. Belief in the value of training has not translated into mandated training requirements.

When the researchers compared state professional development policies against Education Week's 2009 Quality Counts ratings, a pattern emerged: states requiring board professional development received B or C ratings, while states without such requirements received C or D ratings. This is not a dramatic performance gap, and it is a correlation, not a cause — but it is directionally consistent and practically meaningful. States that invest in board training appear to run better-rated education systems. The alternative explanation — that higher-performing states simply happen to adopt more governance policies of all types — cannot be ruled out, but the pattern is worth noting.

Why Most States Still Don't Require It

The disconnect between directors' beliefs and actual state policy is itself a finding worth examining. Professional development requirements for teachers are ubiquitous — continuing education credits, license renewal cycles, and mandatory training hours are standard features of the teaching profession. The same is not true for school board members. Several structural factors likely explain this gap: board members are elected officials rather than employees, creating political and constitutional complications for mandated training; state legislatures are often reluctant to impose requirements on locally elected officials; and there is no credentialing infrastructure for board service comparable to teacher licensure systems.

The result is a governance environment in which the people setting direction for multi-million-dollar public institutions may arrive in the role with no formal preparation whatsoever. Directors who work with boards daily see the consequences and believe training matters. The policy infrastructure to require that training largely does not exist.

Limitations Worth Understanding

This study has real methodological limitations that affect how confidently practitioners should draw conclusions from it. The sample of 26 state directors is small. The comparison of professional development policies to Education Week ratings is correlational — states with board training requirements may differ from states without them in many other ways that drive the rating differences. The study does not control for confounding variables such as state demographics, funding levels, or political culture. And Education Week ratings, while widely used, are themselves a composite measure that blends policy indicators with outcome data in ways that may or may not isolate governance quality.

These limitations do not make the findings useless — they make them suggestive rather than definitive. The study is most valuable as a foundation for further research and as a policy argument: if states that require board professional development are rated higher by a credible external assessor, that is at minimum a reason to examine the practice more closely. It is not a reason to treat board training as a proven lever for student outcomes absent stronger causal evidence.

Implications for Governance Practice

For board members and the organizations that support them, this research reinforces the case for treating board professional development as a structural priority rather than an optional enrichment activity. The pattern in this data is consistent with the broader research literature suggesting that governance quality is not an innate property of elected officials but a developed capacity that requires investment. How boards learn to govern matters — and most governance systems do not take that seriously enough.

  • Training requirements signal governance seriousness: States and districts that formalize professional development expectations for board members send a signal that governing well is a learned skill, not a natural endowment of anyone willing to run for office.
  • Director beliefs represent practitioner wisdom: The near-consensus among state school board association directors that professional development matters should carry weight — these are professionals who observe many boards across many contexts and bring accumulated pattern recognition to their assessments.
  • The gap between belief and policy is a policy lever: If directors widely believe training matters but most states don't require it, the advocacy agenda is clear — the constraint is political will and infrastructure, not evidence of value.
  • Correlation is a starting point, not a conclusion: The positive association between training requirements and Education Week ratings is directionally encouraging but causally underdetermined. Boards should invest in professional development based on its inherent logic — informed governance is better governance — not solely because this study proves an achievement effect.
The Bottom Line

States requiring professional development for school board members receive better external ratings of their education systems than states that don't — a pattern consistent with the view that board training is a genuine governance investment, not a procedural formality. Most states still don't require it, despite broad professional agreement that it matters. For boards and policymakers serious about governance quality, this study provides modest but directionally consistent support for formalizing board development expectations.
