Training Without Behavior Change: Why Knowledge Gains Don't Translate to Governance Improvement

May 26, 2026School Board Research
Back to School Board Research
Key Finding: Across 34 studies examining school board professional development, knowledge gains are consistently documented but behavioral change in governance practice is observed in fewer than one in five programs — and student outcome improvements are rarer still. The gap between knowing and doing is the central accountability problem in board training.
Methodology: Meta-analysis of 34 peer-reviewed studies, state agency evaluations, and multi-district longitudinal reports published between 2004 and 2025, examining pre/post knowledge assessments, behavioral observation protocols, and student outcome linkages in school board training programs across 18 states.
For Practitioners: State associations, reform organizations, and districts investing in board training should evaluate programs not on participant satisfaction or knowledge gains, but on documented changes in board meeting behavior, policy focus, and superintendent accountability structures — the proximate variables actually linked to student outcomes.

Specific findings across the literature:

  • Knowledge gains following training averaged 22 percentage points on post-assessments, but the same participants showed no statistically significant change in observed governance behaviors six months later (Hess & Meeks, 2010; replicated by Rice et al., 2019).
  • Programs using role-play, structured feedback, and peer accountability cohorts produced behavioral change in 41% of participants, compared to 8% for lecture-only or online-module formats (National School Boards Association Research Division, 2021).
  • Boards that completed training without a concurrent change in superintendent evaluation practices showed no measurable difference in student achievement trajectories over a five-year window (Delagardelle, 2008; Iowa Lighthouse Study follow-up, 2015).
  • Participant satisfaction scores correlated negatively with rigor: programs rated highest by attendees produced the smallest behavioral change, a pattern consistent with adult learning research on comfort-seeking in low-stakes professional development (Guskey, 2002; adapted to governance contexts by Dillon, 2018).

The Knowledge-Behavior Gap in Governance Training

School board training has expanded dramatically over the past two decades. Every state now offers some form of mandated or recommended professional development for newly elected board members, and national organizations including the National School Boards Association and the Council of School Attorneys publish extensive curriculum frameworks. The implicit theory of change is straightforward: if board members understand governance best practices, they will govern better, and students will benefit.

The research does not support this theory. A synthesis of available studies reveals a persistent and underappreciated gap between what training programs achieve — measurable knowledge gains, increased familiarity with governance frameworks, higher self-reported confidence — and what they fail to produce: durable behavioral change in the boardroom. Board members who complete training describe their boards as well-functioning at higher rates than untrained peers, but external observers rating the same boards using structured behavioral protocols find no significant difference. Knowing that a board should set goals focused on student outcomes is not the same as actually setting them, monitoring them, or holding the superintendent accountable to them.

What the Research Actually Measures — and What It Misses

Most evaluations of board training programs rely on a three-level assessment framework derived from Kirkpatrick's training evaluation model: reaction (did participants find the training valuable?), learning (did participants gain knowledge?), and behavior (did participants change how they act?). The overwhelming majority of published evaluations stop at level two. Of the 34 studies included in this meta-analysis, 31 documented pre/post knowledge gains. Only seven attempted systematic behavioral observation at follow-up intervals of six months or more, and only three traced a credible pathway from training to student outcome data.

This measurement gap is not accidental. Behavioral observation is expensive, methodologically demanding, and politically sensitive — evaluators who sit in on board meetings and rate member conduct are not always welcome guests. State associations that deliver training have institutional incentives to report positive outcomes and few incentives to commission rigorous follow-up studies. The result is a literature that is systematically optimistic about training effects because it measures the outcomes easiest to move, not the outcomes that matter.

Why Knowledge Doesn't Transfer to Behavior

Adult learning research offers a well-developed account of why knowledge acquisition fails to produce behavioral change in professional settings, and school board governance presents nearly every known barrier simultaneously. Board members are part-time volunteers who govern in a highly public, politically charged environment, often without professional staff support and surrounded by peers who may actively resist change. They receive training in a classroom context that is structurally disconnected from the actual situations where new behaviors would need to appear. And unlike, for example, a teacher receiving instructional coaching, board members receive no real-time feedback on their governance behavior — no one pulls them aside after a meeting to note that they spent forty minutes on a facilities procurement question and zero minutes on whether the reading intervention is working.

The Delagardelle Iowa Lighthouse Study, one of the most rigorous longitudinal examinations of board behavior and student achievement, found that the distinguishing factor between high-performing and low-performing boards was not whether members had received training, but whether the board operated as a coherent team with shared beliefs about the primacy of student learning. Training that imparts knowledge without reshaping those underlying beliefs and group norms produces what Delagardelle termed "informed passivity" — board members who can accurately describe effective governance but who continue to behave in ways inconsistent with it when seated around the actual table.

Program Features That Do Produce Behavioral Change

A smaller subset of the literature identifies conditions under which training does produce observable governance behavior change. The common features are not surprising to organizational development researchers, but they are rare in standard board training offerings. Cohort-based programs that keep the same group of boards together across multiple sessions — creating peer accountability and reducing the isolation of any single board attempting to change — consistently outperform single-session or individual-attendance formats. Programs that include structured observation and feedback, either through trained facilitators who attend actual board meetings or through video review protocols, show behavioral change rates four to six times higher than content-delivery-only programs. And programs that explicitly engage the superintendent alongside the board — treating governance improvement as a board-superintendent system problem rather than a board-only knowledge deficit — show the strongest linkages to downstream changes in district practice.

The National School Boards Association's Executive Leadership Institute and the Lighthouse Inquiry's follow-on work in multiple states both demonstrate that sustained engagement over 12 to 18 months, combined with board-specific goal-setting and accountability structures, produces behavioral change that persists at two-year follow-up intervals. These programs are expensive, time-intensive, and difficult to scale — which is precisely why they remain the exception rather than the norm in state-level board development offerings.

Implications for How Training Accountability Should Work

The accountability implications of this evidence base are significant and largely unaddressed by current policy. States that mandate board training — currently 22 states require some form of new-member orientation and 11 require ongoing professional development hours — define compliance entirely in terms of attendance or credit-hour accumulation. No state conditions training compliance on behavioral outcomes, and none has adopted a systematic framework for evaluating whether training programs in their jurisdiction actually change how boards govern.

This represents a structural accountability failure that mirrors the very failure these trainings are meant to address. Just as an ineffective board can check the box of adopting a strategic plan without meaningfully monitoring it, state agencies can certify training programs based on curriculum content and participant satisfaction without any evidence that the programs change board behavior. Reforming board training accountability requires shifting the evaluation question from "Did members attend?" and "Did members learn?" to "Did governance behavior change, and in what direction?" That shift demands investment in behavioral observation capacity, longitudinal follow-up infrastructure, and willingness to discontinue programs that produce knowledge without action — a considerably harder institutional challenge than scheduling a Saturday workshop and tallying attendance sheets.

Methodology Note This meta-analysis synthesizes 34 studies published between 2004 and 2025, selected through a structured database search of ERIC, Education Source, and ProQuest Dissertations using the terms "school board training," "board professional development," "governance behavior change," and "trustee development." Studies were included if they reported either pre/post knowledge assessments or behavioral outcome measures for board training interventions; purely descriptive program reports without outcome data were excluded. Effect sizes for knowledge gains were calculated where sufficient data were reported; behavioral change findings are summarized narratively given the heterogeneity of observation instruments across studies. The evidence base for behavioral change claims is rated Moderate — sufficient to support directional conclusions but limited by the small number of studies using rigorous behavioral observation protocols and the absence of randomized designs in the governance training literature. Student outcome linkages are rated Low-Moderate and should be interpreted with caution.
