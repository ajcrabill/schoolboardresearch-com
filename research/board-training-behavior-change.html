Core Finding: States requiring professional development for school board members consistently earned B or C ratings from Education Week's annual education system assessment, while states without such requirements tended to receive C or D ratings. Most states currently do not mandate board member training, yet state association directors widely believe such training positively affects student outcomes.
Methodology: Survey of 50 state school board association directors, with responses compared against Education Week's 2009 "Quality Counts" ratings of state education systems. The study combined director perceptions of training impact with an observable policy variable — whether each state mandates professional development — and mapped both against an independent external rating of state education quality.
For Practitioners: Board members and state policymakers should treat professional development not as an optional benefit but as a governance accountability requirement. States that treat training as mandatory infrastructure for board service appear to produce stronger overall education systems than those that leave it to individual discretion.

The Policy Landscape: Voluntary by Default

Despite decades of research linking effective school board governance to student outcomes, the United States has no national requirement that school board members receive professional training before or after taking office. The study by Roberts and Sampson (2011) documents the result: as of 2009, most states did not mandate professional development for board members, leaving training participation to individual motivation and local culture. Board members are elected officials who take on complex governance responsibilities — fiduciary oversight, superintendent hiring and evaluation, policy adoption, budget authority — with no required preparation for any of it.

This voluntary default is not neutral. When training is optional, the board members who most need it are least likely to seek it out, and the boards facing the greatest governance challenges are often the ones with the fewest internal norms supporting ongoing professional development. The Roberts and Sampson survey found that state school board association directors generally believed training produced positive outcomes — but belief in training's value and a policy mandate requiring it are two very different things, and most states had the former without the latter.

What the Education Week Comparison Reveals

The study's most actionable finding comes from comparing states' training mandate status against Education Week's 2009 "Quality Counts" ratings, which assess state education systems across multiple dimensions including standards, assessments, accountability policy, and student achievement data. States that required professional development for board members tended to cluster in the B and C rating range; states without such requirements tended to cluster in the C and D range. This is a correlational finding — not a causal one — but the pattern is consistent and suggestive.

What the correlation plausibly reflects is not that training alone drives state education quality, but that states willing to impose a governance accountability requirement for board members are also states that have invested more broadly in systemic education infrastructure. The mandate is a signal of governance seriousness. States that require board training are likely also states that take accountability policy, data systems, and standards implementation more seriously across the board. The training requirement is both a direct intervention and an indicator of a broader governance culture that prioritizes preparation, accountability, and continuous improvement.

Why Directors Believe Training Matters

The survey's director responses illuminate the practitioner theory of change underlying training advocacy. State school board association directors — who interact with board members across their states, observe governance quality variation, and often deliver training themselves — reported broad agreement that professional development positively affects student outcomes. Their reasoning tended to focus on the mediating role of decision quality: board members who understand curriculum, finance, governance roles, and superintendent evaluation are better positioned to make the decisions that shape district direction. Poor decisions by undertrained boards consume administrative bandwidth, generate legal liability, distract superintendents from instructional leadership, and signal to communities that governance is a low-accountability function.

This theory of change is plausible and consistent with the broader governance literature, but Roberts and Sampson are careful to note that the director survey captures perceptions, not demonstrated causal pathways. The study does not trace a direct line from specific training content to specific board behaviors to specific student outcomes. What it does document is that the people closest to the problem — those who observe boards across entire states over many years — believe the link is real and that mandatory training is a necessary policy response to a governance accountability gap.

Limitations and What the Study Cannot Tell Us

The Roberts and Sampson study is a survey-comparative design, and its evidentiary limitations are real. The sample of 50 directors is complete — all 50 states — but the survey captures perceptions rather than behavioral or outcome data. The Education Week comparison is suggestive but not controlled: states differ on dozens of dimensions that could explain both the presence of training mandates and higher education ratings. The study does not examine what training content is required where mandates exist, how many hours are required, whether quality varies, or whether completion is actually enforced. A mandate on paper that no one monitors may produce little behavioral change regardless of what the policy says.

The 2009 data also predates significant shifts in school board governance reform activity, including the expansion of the Council of the Great City Schools' urban board governance work, the Effective School Boards framework, and post-pandemic governance disruptions that have reshaped what board training addresses. The directional findings remain relevant, but the specific state-by-state policy landscape has evolved since the study was published.

Implications for Governance Practice

The study's core implication is structural: voluntary training will not close the governance preparation gap. State associations, legislatures, and national governance reform organizations should treat professional development as a minimum condition of board service, not a benefit available to the motivated. The analogy to other public roles is direct — judges receive mandatory education, teachers require licensure, public health officials complete required training. School board members make decisions affecting hundreds of thousands of children with no comparable preparation requirement in most states.

  • Mandate, don't recommend: Director belief in training value is widespread, but belief without policy mandate produces uneven participation. States serious about governance quality should require training, not just offer it.
  • Link training to accountability structures: A training requirement without a completion enforcement mechanism replicates the same accountability gap the training is meant to address. Mandates need teeth.
  • Align training content to governance responsibilities: Roberts and Sampson argue board members need development across all areas of public schooling — finance, curriculum, personnel, policy — not just orientation to procedural rules. Breadth of preparation matters.
  • Treat the Education Week pattern as a policy signal: Even absent controlled evidence, the consistent association between training mandates and higher state education ratings is a reason for policymakers in lower-rated states to examine whether governance preparation is part of what they are missing.
The Bottom Line

States that require professional development for school board members show better overall education system ratings than states that leave training voluntary. Most states still do not mandate it. If governing well requires preparation — and the research suggests it does — then treating board member training as optional is a structural accountability failure that states and national reform organizations should move to correct.
