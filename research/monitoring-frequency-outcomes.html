How Boards Actually Process Accountability Policy

Sutherland's 2022 study in Educational Policy addresses a persistent puzzle in governance research: if federal and state accountability frameworks are designed with clear implementation expectations, why does local implementation so often look different from what was intended? The study approaches this question through the lens of sensemaking theory — the idea that organizational actors do not simply receive and execute policy, but actively construct interpretations of it that shape their behavior. Using interviews, observations, and document analysis in a single district during the ESSA transition period, Sutherland found that board members did not passively receive federal and state accountability policy. They actively reinterpreted it through local frames — community priorities, political context, prior experience with accountability regimes — producing narratives that diverged meaningfully from what state and federal policymakers intended. The studied board was not resistant or uninformed; members were engaged and articulate about accountability. But their engagement took the form of translation, not transmission.

This finding situates boards within a much larger body of policy implementation research showing that local actors — teachers, principals, administrators — adapt external mandates through existing cognitive and institutional schemas. Sutherland's contribution is to demonstrate that the same dynamic operates at the board level, which prior research had largely left unexamined.

The Data-Use Gap

The study's second key finding is more pointed and more immediately actionable for governance practitioners. Board members described their relationship to assessment data in terms of accountability: they said they used data to hold the district accountable, to track whether students were meeting expectations, to evaluate superintendent performance. This is the right answer — it reflects how governance literature and state policy frameworks say boards should use data.

But Sutherland found that what board members described and what they actually did were different. In practice, the board used assessment data primarily for local legitimacy management — presenting data to the community in ways that reinforced confidence in district leadership, selecting metrics that reflected favorably on local programs, and framing accountability language in terms of community values rather than state benchmarks. The data were used to tell a story to constituents, not to drive internal accountability decisions.

This gap between stated and actual data use is not unique to the studied district — it echoes findings from research on how data moves through organizations more broadly. What makes it significant for governance practitioners is the specific form it takes at the board level: boards occupy the accountability apex of the district. If the accountability function erodes into legitimacy management at that layer, the signal does not travel down the organizational chain.

What Boards Trying to Implement Accountability Policy Should Know

The study's single-district qualitative design limits how directly its findings can be exported to other contexts. The specific narrative patterns Sutherland documents are illustrative, not representative. But the underlying dynamic — that boards will translate external accountability frameworks into locally-coherent narratives, and that stated data use and actual data use may diverge — is theoretically grounded and plausible across governance contexts. Practitioners should treat this study as a diagnostic provocation rather than a prescriptive manual.

  • Audit the gap between stated and actual data use: The finding that board members described accountability-oriented data use while practicing legitimacy-oriented data use is a governance quality issue, not an academic abstraction. Boards and governance coaches should examine the actual record of how assessment data has been referenced in board deliberations, policy decisions, and superintendent direction-setting — not survey members about their philosophy of data use.
  • Treat accountability translation as inevitable, not as failure: Boards will adapt state and federal accountability messaging into locally meaningful narratives. State education agencies should treat this as a design constraint to work with rather than a compliance failure to prevent. Communication strategies that explain the reasoning behind accountability metrics — not just the metrics themselves — are more likely to produce local translations that stay aligned with the intent of the framework.
  • Distinguish legitimate local adaptation from accountability drift: Not all sensemaking divergence represents misalignment. Boards that connect state accountability metrics to community-specific goals for students may be doing governance work that strengthens rather than undermines accountability. The diagnostic question is whether the local narrative preserves the underlying accountability logic or quietly replaces it with something that serves different purposes.
  • Use ESSA's flexibility as an engagement opportunity: ESSA's move toward state-designed accountability frameworks creates conditions for more authentic board engagement with accountability design. Boards that actively learn how their state's framework was constructed — and why particular indicators were chosen — are better positioned to make sense of it in governance-aligned ways than boards that receive the framework as an external mandate to be managed.
The Bottom Line

Sutherland's study provides credible qualitative evidence that school board members are active sensemakers who adapt federal and state accountability messaging into local narratives, and that a meaningful gap can exist between their stated use of assessment data and their actual use of it in governance. The findings are theoretically grounded, methodologically appropriate to the research question, and connected to an established body of policy implementation research. The single-district design limits claims about prevalence, but the dynamic the study identifies — local narrative construction at the board level, and data deployed for legitimacy rather than accountability — is a pattern that boards and state policymakers should treat as a live diagnostic question in their own contexts.
