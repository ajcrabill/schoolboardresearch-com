Core Finding: Board members did not passively receive federal and state accountability policy — they actively interpreted it through local frames, producing narratives that diverged from policymakers' intent. The study also found a gap between how participants described their relationship to assessment data (as a tool for accountability) and how they actually used it in practice (as a tool for local legitimacy management). These two divergences together suggest that implementation fidelity to accountability frameworks at the board level is more contested than federal and state architects typically assume.
Methodology: Qualitative case study design using interviews, document analysis, and observations with school board members in a single district. Data collection examined how board members made sense of federal accountability policies (principally under ESSA's transition from NCLB) and how that sensemaking shaped their use of assessment data as a governance instrument.
For Practitioners: State education agencies and federal policymakers should expect that accountability messaging will be locally translated rather than locally implemented — and that the translation process is consequential. Boards and governance coaches should treat the gap between a board's stated use of data and its actual use of data as a diagnostic target: the gap is common, it has roots in sensemaking theory, and it is addressable through structured governance processes that make data use explicit rather than assumed.

What This Study Set Out to Do

Daniella Hall Sutherland's 2022 study in Educational Policy addresses a persistent puzzle in governance research: if federal and state accountability frameworks are designed with clear implementation expectations, why does local implementation so often look different from what was intended? The study approaches this question through the lens of sensemaking theory — the idea that organizational actors do not simply receive and execute policy, but actively construct interpretations of it that shape their behavior. For a full explanation of the PEARLS framework used in this review, see our methodology page.

The study focuses specifically on school board members — a governance layer that accountability research has often treated as a passive conduit between state mandates and district administration. Sutherland challenges this assumption directly: board members, the study argues, are active sensemakers who mediate between external accountability demands and local governance behavior. Under the Every Student Succeeds Act's transition toward state-designed accountability frameworks, understanding how boards make sense of those frameworks is not an academic curiosity — it has direct implications for how state education agencies should design and communicate policy.

E — Evidence Quality

The study uses a qualitative case study design — appropriate given the research question, which asks how board members make sense of policy rather than whether sensemaking produces measurable outcomes. Sensemaking research demands access to how actors construct meaning, which requires interview data, observation, and document analysis rather than statistical modeling. The methodological choice matches the inquiry.

The central limitation of the evidence is its single-district scope. Case study research generates deep, contextually rich data, but that depth comes at the cost of sample breadth. The study cannot tell us how widespread the patterns it identifies are — whether the sensemaking gaps it documents are typical of school boards broadly, characteristic of a particular board type, or artifacts of the specific district's context. Sutherland does not claim broad representativeness, but the limitation constrains how far the findings can travel.

Within the case, the evidence quality appears appropriate: multiple data sources (interviews, observation, documents) allow for triangulation, which strengthens credibility for qualitative work. Sensemaking as a theoretical framework is well-established in organizational studies and has been applied productively to education policy contexts, so the interpretive lens is not ad hoc. The study's key claims — that participants' stated interpretations diverged from their actual data use, and that they adapted external messaging into local narratives — are the kind of findings that triangulated qualitative data can legitimately support. What it cannot support is any estimate of how often this pattern occurs across districts nationally.

A — Applicability

Applicability is this study's most significant constraint. A single-district case study provides rich interpretive data but offers limited direct transfer to governance practitioners in different contexts. Districts vary enormously in their board cultures, policy environments, local political dynamics, and relationships to state accountability systems. The sensemaking patterns Sutherland documents in one district may or may not reflect how board members in other districts — different sizes, demographics, states, and accountability frameworks — construct meaning from federal and state policy.

That said, the study's theoretical contribution travels more broadly than its sample does. Sensemaking theory as applied here is not dependent on particular district characteristics — it is a general account of how organizational actors process ambiguous external demands. The claim that board members will adapt external accountability messaging into locally-coherent narratives rather than accepting it as designed is plausible across governance contexts, even if the specific form that adaptation takes will vary by district. Practitioners and state education agencies should treat the specific narrative patterns Sutherland identifies as illustrative rather than representative, while treating the underlying dynamic — local narrative construction as a governance behavior — as a reasonable working hypothesis for their own contexts.

Practitioners asking "what would my board do with this?" should use this study as a provocation rather than a manual: it raises diagnostic questions about the gap between stated and actual data use that any board can investigate, even if the answers will be locally specific.

R — Replicability

The qualitative design raises inherent questions about replicability in the conventional sense. The methodology is described sufficiently to allow a researcher to conduct a parallel case study in another district using the same theoretical framework, but the findings would not be expected to replicate precisely — they would be expected to corroborate or challenge the sensemaking patterns identified here. This is the appropriate standard for qualitative research, and Sutherland's study meets it.

Corroborating literature exists. The broader sensemaking tradition in education — developed through Weick's (1995) foundational organizational work and extended to education policy contexts by researchers including Spillane (2004) and Coburn (2001, 2006) — provides a well-established theoretical home for these findings. Spillane's work on how teachers and administrators make sense of reform policy, in particular, identified similar dynamics: interpreters adapt external policy through existing cognitive schemas, producing local translations that may diverge substantially from policy authors' intent. Sutherland's contribution extends this tradition to the board level, which has received less systematic attention. The finding that board members adapted external accountability messaging into local narratives aligns with the broader pattern of local sensemaking documented across the governance and implementation literature, lending it more credibility than a purely isolated finding would carry.

L — Limitations

The study's authors presumably acknowledge the standard case study limitation: generalizability is constrained, and the findings should not be extended beyond the studied context without additional evidence. This is the expected limitation statement for case study research, and it is accurate as far as it goes.

Several additional limitations merit attention that the study may not fully foreground. First, single-district qualitative research is subject to selection effects at the site level: boards that agreed to participate in a study of their accountability sensemaking may systematically differ from boards that would not. If the studied board was relatively reflective about its own governance practices — a precondition for granting research access — the findings may overstate the degree to which board members elsewhere engage consciously with sensemaking processes at all.

Second, the temporal context is worth interrogating carefully. The ESSA transition period (post-2015) created a specific kind of policy ambiguity — states were designing new accountability frameworks as board members were simultaneously attempting to make sense of what accountability would mean going forward. This is an unusually high-ambiguity environment for sensemaking research. Whether the same patterns persist once state accountability frameworks have stabilized and become routine is an open question the study cannot answer.

Third, qualitative research relying on interview data is subject to social desirability effects: board members may describe their data use in ways that align with what they believe is expected of effective governance, rather than what they actually do. The triangulation with observation and document data helps, but the gap between stated interpretations and actual use that the study identifies could itself be partially a measurement artifact — different data collection methods may surface different registers of sensemaking, not necessarily evidence of genuine behavioral inconsistency.

S — Significance

This study's most significant contribution is the board-level application of sensemaking theory to federal accountability policy. Prior implementation research has extensively documented how administrators and teachers translate policy, but school boards have rarely been studied as active sensemakers in this tradition. Sutherland closes that gap — and the finding that boards construct local narratives from federal frameworks is consequential for how state education agencies design accountability communication, how federal agencies calibrate implementation expectations, and how governance researchers model policy transmission from state to district.

The study is best understood as an extending contribution: it applies an established theoretical framework to a governance layer where that framework had not yet been systematically applied. It does not overturn prior research; it deepens and extends it. For practitioners, the significance is proportionate to the evidence: the study raises the right diagnostic questions about board sensemaking without providing prescriptive answers that its single-district design cannot support. State education agencies designing ESSA implementation support should take seriously the finding that accountability messaging will be locally translated — and invest accordingly in communication and professional development strategies that work with local sensemaking rather than assuming passive policy receipt.

Implications for Governance Practice

The practical implications of this study operate at two levels: what state and federal actors should expect from boards, and what boards should expect from themselves. Both levels point toward the same underlying recommendation: make sensemaking explicit rather than leaving it to operate invisibly in board deliberations.

  • Audit the gap between stated and actual data use: Sutherland's finding that board members' stated interpretations of assessment data diverged from how they used it in practice is a governance quality issue, not merely an academic observation. Boards and governance coaches should design structured processes that make data use explicit — not asking board members to describe their philosophy of data use, but examining the actual record of how assessment data has been referenced in board deliberations, policy decisions, and superintendent direction-setting.
  • Treat accountability translation as inevitable, not as failure: Boards will construct local narratives from state and federal accountability frameworks. State education agencies should treat this as a design constraint to work with, not a compliance failure to prevent. Communication strategies that provide interpretive scaffolding — explaining the reasoning behind accountability metrics, not just reporting them — are more likely to produce locally-adapted narratives that remain aligned with the intent of the framework.
  • Distinguish legitimate local adaptation from drift: Not all sensemaking adaptation represents misalignment. Boards that adapt state accountability language into locally meaningful frameworks — connecting state metrics to the community's specific goals for students — may be doing governance work that strengthens rather than undermines accountability. The diagnostic question is whether the local narrative preserves the underlying accountability logic or replaces it with something that serves different purposes.
  • Use ESSA's flexibility as a sensemaking opportunity, not just a policy tool: ESSA's move toward state-designed accountability frameworks creates the conditions for more authentic board engagement with accountability design. Boards that participate actively in understanding how their state's framework was designed — and why particular indicators were chosen — are better positioned to make sense of it in governance-aligned ways than boards that receive the framework as an external mandate.
The Bottom Line

Sutherland's study provides credible qualitative evidence that school board members are active sensemakers who adapt federal and state accountability messaging into local narratives, and that a gap frequently exists between their stated interpretations of assessment data and their actual use of it in governance. The findings are theoretically grounded, methodologically appropriate to the research question, and situated within an established body of policy implementation research. The single-district case study design limits claims about prevalence, but the underlying dynamic the study identifies — local narrative construction as a board governance behavior — is a plausible and practically important pattern that state policymakers and governance practitioners should factor into their work.
