Core Finding: State takeover of underperforming school districts does not generate academic benefits on average. Across all takeovers nationally from the late 1980s through 2016, the researchers found no positive effect on student achievement, with early-year disruption producing measurable harm to English Language Arts outcomes in particular. Results varied significantly by context: takeovers were least effective in districts with higher baseline achievement and produced the least harm in majority-Latinx communities.
Methodology: The authors conducted a quasi-experimental national analysis tracking all state takeovers of underperforming school districts from the late 1980s through 2016. Using a difference-in-differences framework, they compared achievement trajectories in taken-over districts against comparable non-taken-over districts, controlling for prior achievement levels and district demographics. The study also examined heterogeneity in effects by district racial composition and baseline achievement, and modeled what factors predicted which districts were selected for takeover.
For Practitioners: School board members, state policymakers, and community advocates should understand that the evidence base does not support takeover as a reliable improvement strategy — particularly in majority-African American districts, where the research also finds that academic performance plays a smaller role in the takeover decision itself. Boards can use this research to inform advocacy positions and to demand rigorous evaluation plans before any state intervention proceeds.

The National Takeover Record

State takeover of local school districts has been one of the most dramatic governance interventions available to state governments since the late 1980s. The premise is straightforward: when a district persistently underperforms and local governance is deemed unable to self-correct, the state assumes direct control — replacing elected boards, appointing emergency managers or state-designated superintendents, and in some cases dissolving or restructuring the district entirely. The policy has been applied in cities ranging from Newark and Detroit to New Orleans and Oakland, and it carries enormous symbolic and political weight as a signal that failure will not be tolerated.

Schueler and Bleiberg bring unusual methodological rigor to this record by tracking every state takeover nationally across nearly three decades rather than focusing on a single high-profile case. The result is the most comprehensive picture yet of what takeover actually produces at scale — and the answer is sobering. Across the full population of takeovers, there is no evidence of academic benefit on average. The policy's intuitive appeal as an accountability backstop has not translated into measurable gains for students.

Why Disruption Matters

The study's finding about early-year disruption is particularly important for practitioners to understand. Even if takeover were eventually beneficial — a claim the data do not support — the transition period itself produces measurable harm, particularly to English Language Arts achievement. This disruption effect is not merely a statistical artifact: it reflects real dynamics that governance intervention researchers have documented in other contexts. Leadership turnover destabilizes instructional programs. Staff uncertainty affects classroom climate. Community resistance can reduce family engagement. The operational demands of a governance transition compete directly with the operational demands of running schools well.

For policymakers, this means the case for takeover must clear a higher bar than simply "the district is failing." The intervention must be expected to produce benefits large enough to offset early disruption costs — and the evidence suggests that bar is rarely cleared. For school board members in districts that might be subject to takeover consideration, the research provides a principled basis for arguing that the costs of intervention are real and the benefits are not.

Race, Politics, and the Takeover Decision

One of the study's most significant findings concerns the factors that predict takeover itself, not just its effects. The researchers found that academic performance plays a smaller role in predicting takeover for districts serving larger concentrations of African American students. This is a striking result: the policy ostensibly operates on academic grounds, but the evidence suggests that racial demographics modulate the threshold at which intervention is triggered. Districts with more African American students face higher risk of takeover at equivalent — or even better — performance levels compared to districts with different demographic profiles.

This finding is not merely a critique of past policy — it is a structural warning about how state intervention decisions are made. If takeover is applied in ways that are not tightly coupled to academic need, then the policy becomes less about improving outcomes and more about political and demographic dynamics that its proponents rarely acknowledge. The implications for governance equity are significant: the communities least likely to benefit from takeover (majority-African American districts, per the heterogeneity findings) may also be the communities most likely to experience it.

Heterogeneity: Context Shapes Outcomes

The study's heterogeneity analysis adds nuance that average effects can obscure. Takeovers were least effective in districts with higher baseline achievement — a finding that may reflect a floor effect, since districts already performing at higher levels have less room to improve and may also be less amenable to the kinds of structural disruption takeover produces. The finding that majority-Latinx communities experienced the least harm is harder to interpret causally but may reflect differences in how takeover was implemented in those contexts, the nature of district relationships with state oversight structures, or community response dynamics.

The central lesson from heterogeneity analysis is that "state takeover" is not a single, uniform intervention — it is a category of interventions that varies substantially in form, implementation, political context, and community characteristics. Average effects that find no benefit mask variation that might, in some narrow set of circumstances, produce positive results. But that variation cuts both ways: it also masks takeovers that were seriously harmful. Policymakers who select favorable cases to justify the policy are drawing from a distribution whose average is null and whose tails include real harm.

Implications for Governance Practice

For elected school board members, this research has immediate relevance in two directions. First, boards in states where takeover legislation exists should understand the empirical record and factor it into their public positions on state intervention policy. Advocating for outcome-based accountability without a clear-eyed assessment of what the available interventions actually accomplish is not responsible governance. Second, boards that receive signals of potential state intervention — through state report cards, corrective action notices, or legislative attention — can use this research to frame conversations about what effective support looks like versus what the evidence suggests structural intervention produces.

  • Takeover is not an accountability shortcut: The lack of average academic benefit means that state takeover cannot be treated as a reliable escalation path when districts fail to improve — it shifts governance without reliably producing the outcomes that justify the shift.
  • Early disruption is a documented cost: Boards and community members have standing to raise disruption costs as a real factor in intervention decisions, not merely as resistance to accountability. The research supports that framing.
  • Demand evidence-based alternatives: If state takeover does not improve outcomes on average, states and districts should redirect energy toward interventions with stronger evidence bases — capacity-building, leadership development, governance coaching, and targeted support that do not require stripping local control.
  • Racial equity demands scrutiny of takeover triggers: The finding that African American student concentration predicts takeover independent of performance levels is a governance equity issue that boards, advocacy organizations, and policymakers should address explicitly rather than treat as background noise in accountability design.
  • Context-sensitivity is required: Any district or state contemplating intervention should commission rigorous evaluation from the start, pre-specify success metrics, and build in honest review mechanisms — rather than treating governance transfer as self-justifying.
The Bottom Line

Three decades of state takeover data produce a clear verdict: on average, removing local governance control does not improve student achievement, often disrupts it in the short term, and appears to be applied inequitably across racial demographics. School board members, state policymakers, and community advocates should treat this research as a strong signal that takeover is a costly and unreliable tool — and that the communities asked to absorb its risks deserve evidence-based alternatives, not governance theater.

Verdict This is a high-quality national study with a strong methodological design and near-complete population coverage. Practitioners and policymakers can rely on its central findings — that takeover produces no average academic benefit and may harm early outcomes — as among the most credible evidence available on this question. The scope limitation around longer-term and non-achievement outcomes means it should be read alongside research on fiscal and organizational effects of state intervention, but on the achievement question specifically, this study carries substantial weight.
