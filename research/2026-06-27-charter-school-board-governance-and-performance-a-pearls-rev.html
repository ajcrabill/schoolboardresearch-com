Charter school board governance and performance: A PEARLS Review

June 23, 2026
Study Under Review:
Ford, M. R. (2020). Connecting group dynamics, governance, and performance: Evidence from charter school boards. *Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly, 49*(4), 701–720. https://doi.org/10.1177/0899764020911206

Study Under Review: Ford, M. R. (2020). Connecting group dynamics, governance, and performance: Evidence from charter school boards. Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly, 49(4), 701–720.

This study investigates how group dynamics among charter school board members relate to governance practices and, ultimately, organizational performance. The research addresses a gap in the nonprofit governance literature by examining internal board functioning rather than focusing solely on structural arrangements. Given the increasing prevalence of charter schools in the American educational field and the limited empirical work on charter board effectiveness, this article offers a timely contribution. For a full explanation of the PEARLS framework used in this review, see our [methodology page](/methodology.html).

P — Peer Review

The article appears in Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly, a peer-reviewed journal published by SAGE that occupies a respected position in nonprofit management scholarship. The journal employs standard double-blind peer review, which provides reasonable assurance of methodological scrutiny, though the quality of review varies across manuscripts. The article's author has published related work on school board governance, suggesting domain expertise. However, the peer review process cannot verify the accuracy of self-reported survey data or the appropriateness of statistical models—these remain the author's responsibility. The venue lends credibility but does not guarantee the study's findings are definitive.

E — Evidence Quality

The study uses a cross-sectional quantitative design combining survey data from charter school board members with objective performance indicators. This approach represents a methodological strength: the author links self-reported governance practices and group dynamics to measurable outcomes including student achievement and organizational metrics, moving beyond purely perceptual dependent variables.

However, several concerns merit attention. The cross-sectional design prevents causal inference—while the study finds associations between group dynamics, governance practices, and performance, it cannot establish that positive group dynamics cause better performance or vice versa. The temporal ordering of variables remains ambiguous. Sample composition matters here; the study draws exclusively from charter school boards, which operate under different regulatory frameworks and accountability structures than traditional public school boards. This limits external validity. Additionally, the survey data rely on board members' self-reports, introducing potential response bias where participants may present their boards in favorable terms. The multivariate regression models include covariates, which is appropriate, but unmeasured confounders—such as the quality of school leadership or local policy environments—may drive both group dynamics and performance outcomes.

A — Applicability

The findings apply specifically to charter school boards in the United States. Generalizing to traditional public school district boards requires caution. Charter boards differ meaningfully: they often have smaller membership, operate with greater autonomy from district oversight, and face distinct accountability pressures from authorizers. A rural Minnesota school board functioning within a traditional district structure operates under different dynamics than the charter boards surveyed here.

The study's focus on group dynamics—cohesion, trust, and constructive conflict among board members—may translate more readily than specific governance indices. However, the composite measures used may not capture the full complexity of board functioning in different contexts. Practitioners should consider whether their board's composition, regulatory environment, and stakeholder expectations align with the charter school context before applying these findings directly.

R — Replicability

The peer-reviewed literature directly replicating or contradicting Ford (2020) on charter school board group dynamics, governance, and performance is very limited. No clear, direct replication using the same constructs and design appears in the available evidence.

Related work by Ford and Ihrke examines governing dynamics in traditional public school districts rather than charter schools, providing conceptual support for the importance of board group dynamics but not testing the same governance-performance link in charter contexts. Ford's earlier dissertation on board governance and academic outcomes in traditional public schools supports similar mechanisms but cannot serve as a replication given the different governance context and non-peer-reviewed status. No peer-reviewed studies explicitly contradict Ford (2020) by showing that board group dynamics are unrelated to charter school performance.

The absence of direct replication evidence means the study's findings remain preliminary. Methodological transparency in the article—survey instruments, regression specifications, and sample descriptions—would help future replication attempts, though the specific charter board sample limits this possibility.

L — Limitations

The authors acknowledge several limitations. The cross-sectional design limits causal inference, a point the author appropriately notes. Self-report survey data may introduce response bias or social desirability effects. The sample restricted to charter school boards limits generalizability to traditional public school boards. Composite indices may not capture all complexities of governance and group dynamics. Unmeasured confounders such as leadership quality or local policy context may influence results.

Beyond these stated limitations, additional concerns emerge. The sample size and response rate are not fully transparent in this review, which affects confidence in statistical estimates. The cross-sectional design also means the study cannot address whether board dynamics and governance practices remain stable over time or whether they fluctuate with leadership changes or external pressures. The performance measures, while objective, may not capture the full range of outcomes that matter for students and communities. Selection effects likely operate: charter schools with stronger performance may attract more capable board members, creating a spurious association between group dynamics and outcomes.

S — Significance

This study extends the nonprofit governance literature by examining internal board functioning—group dynamics and governance practices—rather than focusing exclusively on formal structures or external accountability. The finding that positive group dynamics associate with stronger governance and better performance adds to a growing body of work suggesting that how board members interact matters for organizational effectiveness.

However, the significance should not be overstated. The cross-sectional design limits causal claims, and the charter-specific sample restricts generalizability. The study confirms intuitions about board teamwork rather than establishing new causal mechanisms. Its greatest value lies in providing empirical evidence for a commonly assumed relationship and in offering survey instruments that other researchers might adapt for different contexts. For school board practitioners, the study offers suggestive guidance rather than proven best practices.

Practitioner Takeaways

Practitioners can reasonably conclude that board cohesion, trust, and constructive conflict appear associated with stronger governance practices and better organizational outcomes in charter school settings. Boards should attend to how members work together, not just what decisions they make. The finding that a balance between supporting and holding school leadership accountable relates to performance offers a practical framework for board self-assessment.

Practitioners should not conclude that improving group dynamics will definitively improve student outcomes—the study demonstrates association, not causation. They should also avoid applying these findings to traditional public school boards without considering contextual differences. The cross-sectional design means boards cannot assume that mimicking the practices of high-performing charter boards will produce similar results.

Actionable steps include: assessing board role clarity and strategic focus through periodic self-evaluation; measuring trust and conflict resolution patterns among board members; and explicitly balancing support for school leadership with accountability mechanisms. Given the evidence quality, practitioners should weight these findings as suggestive rather than definitive guidance.
