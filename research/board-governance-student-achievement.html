Core Finding: Districts with higher student achievement have school boards that score significantly higher on the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey (EBLPS). Factor analysis revealed six underlying dimensions of board leadership behavior, and a t-test confirmed that high-achieving districts differ meaningfully from low-achieving districts on this composite measure. The study provides early empirical grounding for the proposition that specific, observable board behaviors are associated with better student outcomes.
Methodology: Johnson developed and validated the 33-item Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey (EBLPS) through a three-stage process: content validity (grounded in a literature review identifying 12 board leadership practices), construct validity (via exploratory factor analysis yielding six factors), and predictive validity (via independent-samples t-test comparing EBLPS scores between high- and low-achieving school districts as classified by state accountability data).
For Practitioners: This study gives board members and superintendents a concrete, research-grounded list of practices to benchmark against. The six-factor structure can function as a self-assessment framework: boards that want to improve student outcomes can identify which dimensions they are weakest on and target professional development accordingly. The EBLPS instrument itself may be useful for boards seeking structured reflection on their own governance behaviors.

What the Study Did and Why It Matters

A persistent challenge in school board research is the lack of validated instruments for measuring board behavior. Much of the literature on boards and student achievement relies on self-report surveys of uncertain reliability, administrator perceptions, or indirect proxies such as board tenure or turnover. Johnson's study addresses this gap directly by constructing and rigorously validating a purpose-built survey instrument — the EBLPS — specifically designed to capture the leadership practices of boards that support student achievement.

The instrument's development began with a systematic literature review that identified 12 board leadership practices with prior empirical or theoretical support. These 12 practices were operationalized into 33 survey items, which were then tested for construct validity using factor analysis. The resulting six-factor structure offers a more parsimonious and empirically grounded account of board leadership than the original 12-practice framework, and it provides a foundation for future research that builds on a common measurement base.

The Six Factors of Effective Board Leadership

While Johnson's paper presents the full factor loadings, the six factors that emerged from the analysis represent meaningfully distinct clusters of board behavior. Practitioners can think of them as the primary dimensions along which board performance varies. Boards that score high across all six factors are, according to this study, more likely to be found in high-achieving districts.

  • Vision and goal-setting: The extent to which the board establishes clear, measurable academic goals and communicates a coherent vision for student success across the district community.
  • Policy coherence: Whether board policies are aligned with the district's academic priorities, regularly reviewed, and deliberately designed to support instructional improvement rather than just operational management.
  • Superintendent support and accountability: The board's capacity to maintain productive, professional relationships with the superintendent — providing support while also holding leadership accountable for results.
  • Data use and monitoring: The degree to which the board regularly reviews student outcome data, asks substantive questions about performance trends, and uses evidence to drive its governance decisions.
  • Community engagement: Active and strategic outreach to families and the broader community to build understanding of and support for the district's academic mission.
  • Board development and self-assessment: Whether the board invests in its own professional learning, conducts regular self-evaluations, and treats governance improvement as an ongoing institutional priority.

Validity Evidence and Statistical Findings

The EBLPS demonstrated adequate content validity through its grounding in the prior literature, construct validity through factor analysis, and — most consequentially — predictive validity through the t-test comparing high- and low-achieving districts. The statistically significant difference in mean EBLPS scores between these two groups is the study's central empirical claim: boards in high-achieving districts are, on average, demonstrably different in their self-reported leadership behaviors than boards in low-achieving districts.

It is worth noting that the study relies on self-report survey data completed by board members themselves, which introduces potential social-desirability bias. Districts were classified as high- or low-achieving using state accountability metrics, which vary in design and rigor across states. These methodological features do not invalidate the findings, but they do set appropriate limits on causal inference — the EBLPS distinguishes high- from low-achieving boards, but does not establish that the board behaviors caused the achievement differences.

Implications for Governance Practice

The practical value of this study lies in its operationalization of board effectiveness. For too long, discussions of board improvement have relied on vague appeals to "good governance" without specifying what that looks like in practice. The EBLPS provides a set of behavioral anchors that boards can use for self-assessment, strategic planning, and professional development design. State associations, regional service agencies, and district leadership teams may find the six-factor framework particularly useful for structuring board retreats and governance audits.

  • Use the six factors as a diagnostic lens: Boards can administer the EBLPS or use its conceptual framework to identify which dimensions of their practice are strongest and which need investment.
  • Prioritize data use and goal-setting: These factors tend to be most directly linked to instructional improvement in the broader literature, making them high-leverage starting points for boards that need to sequence their development work.
  • Board self-assessment is not optional: The factor on board development and self-evaluation signals that high-performing boards treat their own improvement as a governance responsibility, not an afterthought.
  • Superintendent partnership is a measurable practice: The board-superintendent relationship factor underscores that effective oversight is not adversarial — it is a collaborative discipline that can be strengthened through intentional practice.
The Bottom Line

Johnson's study provides one of the more rigorous empirical confirmations that school board behavior — not just structure or composition — is measurably associated with student achievement. The six-factor model gives practitioners a concrete, validated framework for assessing and improving board performance. While the cross-sectional survey design limits causal claims, the findings are consistent with the broader governance literature and offer a useful, research-grounded tool for boards committed to improving their practice.
