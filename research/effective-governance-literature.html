States requiring professional development earned higher Education Week ratings (B or C) compared to states without such requirements (C or D), indicating a meaningful association between training mandates and educational system quality.
Methodology:Mixed methods survey study: a questionnaire was sent to 50 directors of state school board associations, with 26 responding (52% response rate). Inductive analysis of qualitative responses was combined with a comparative analysis using Education Week's 2009 state education ratings.
For Practitioners:States and districts should consider mandating structured professional development for school board members, as the evidence suggests this is associated with stronger overall educational outcomes — yet most states currently leave such training voluntary.

Background and Research Gap

School board members wield enormous formal authority over public education — setting policy, approving budgets, hiring superintendents, and establishing district direction — yet they arrive at these roles through popular election rather than credentialing. Unlike virtually every other role in a school system, board membership carries no required training, no demonstrated competency, and no continuing education mandate in most states. The result is a governance layer that is simultaneously powerful and often unprepared.

Roberts and Sampson set out to examine whether this gap matters. Prior research on school board effectiveness had largely sidestepped the question of board member preparation, focusing instead on board-superintendent relations, board composition, or policy adoption. This study specifically asks whether professional development — structured training in governance, finance, law, and educational leadership — has a measurable relationship with student achievement at the state level.

What the Study Did

The researchers developed and distributed a questionnaire to all 50 directors of state school board associations, who serve as the closest thing to authoritative observers of board training culture in each state. Twenty-six directors responded, yielding a 52% response rate. The survey probed whether states required professional development, what forms it took, and whether directors believed such training influenced student outcomes.

Responses were analyzed inductively — the researchers coded themes from the qualitative answers rather than testing a predetermined hypothesis. These findings were then compared against Education Week's 2009 Quality Counts ratings, which grade state education systems on multiple dimensions including standards, accountability, and student achievement. This external benchmark gave the study a comparative anchor that personal opinions alone could not provide.

Key Findings

  • Most states do not require training: The majority of responding state directors reported that their states had no mandatory professional development requirements for school board members, meaning elected members can serve without any formal preparation.
  • Directors believe training matters: Despite the lack of mandates, state directors broadly expressed the view that professional development has a positive effect on student achievement — suggesting a field-level perception that preparation is valuable even where it is not compelled.
  • States with requirements score higher: States that did require professional development for board members tended to receive Education Week ratings of B or C, while states without requirements clustered at C or D — a pattern consistent with the directors' perceptions.
  • A significant research gap persists: The authors note that very little empirical research has been conducted on this topic. Most governance research examines what boards do, not how prepared they are to do it — this study begins to address that blind spot.

Implications for Governance Practice

  • Mandate, don't merely offer: Voluntary training programs reach the already-motivated. If professional development is positively associated with system quality, states and associations should consider requiring it — with meaningful content standards, not just attendance checkboxes.
  • Treat board members as learners, not just leaders: The governance role requires knowledge of finance, policy, law, human resources, and educational research. None of this is intuitive, and the tradition of treating elected status as sufficient preparation is at odds with how every other role in education is structured.
  • Use association infrastructure: State school board associations are already positioned to deliver training. The question is whether states will use their authority to make participation expected rather than optional.
  • Build on this baseline: This study establishes an important correlational pattern, but more rigorous research — with finer-grained outcome measures and controls for state-level confounders — would strengthen the case for policy action.
The Bottom Line

School board members are among the least credentialed actors in a system that otherwise demands licensure and continuing education at every level. Roberts and Sampson's work provides early evidence that this matters — states requiring board training score better on independent education quality ratings. While the study cannot isolate training as the causal driver, the directional signal is clear: preparing the people who govern public schools is not a luxury, it is a condition of responsible governance.

Published in a peer-reviewed international management journal. Mixed-methods design is appropriate for exploratory work. However, the inductive analysis lacks intercoder reliability reporting, and the survey instrument is not reproduced.

Covers 26 states via a single survey round. No longitudinal dimension, no district-level analysis, and no differentiation by type or intensity of professional development. The study is best understood as foundational rather than definitive.
VerdictThis is a useful early-stage study that surfaces an important pattern and legitimizes professional development as a research priority. Practitioners should take the directional finding seriously — especially the contrast between states that require training and those that do not — while recognizing that the study's design cannot rule out confounding explanations. It is a strong argument for investing in rigorous follow-up research, and a reasonable basis for advocating training mandates alongside other evidence.
