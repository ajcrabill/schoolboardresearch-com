Core Finding: Factor analysis of the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey (EBLPS) identified six underlying dimensions of board governance, and t-test comparisons confirmed that districts with higher student achievement consistently scored higher on these factors than lower-achieving districts. The study provides a validated instrument for measuring governance quality rather than relying on informal or unstandardized assessments.
Methodology: A literature review identified 12 board leadership practices associated with student achievement, which were operationalized into a 33-item survey instrument. Responses were subjected to exploratory factor analysis, which reduced the items to six meaningful underlying constructs. T-tests then compared instrument scores between districts grouped by student achievement levels, testing whether high-achieving districts differed systematically from lower-achieving ones.
For Practitioners: The EBLPS provides boards and governance improvement programs with a structured, research-grounded way to assess current practice against the factors most associated with student achievement. Districts can use the instrument diagnostically — not just as a score but as a map of which governance dimensions may need strengthening.

Why Instrument Validation Matters for Governance Research

One of the persistent methodological weaknesses in school board governance research is measurement. Studies often assert that certain board practices correlate with student outcomes, but the measurement of those practices — what counts as "goal-setting," what counts as "monitoring" — is frequently ad hoc, unstandardized, or borrowed from corporate governance frameworks with limited applicability to public education. Johnson's work addresses this gap directly by building and validating a survey instrument grounded explicitly in the K-12 governance literature.

The validation process matters because it distinguishes between constructs that researchers believe exist and constructs that the data confirm exist as coherent, distinct dimensions. The fact that factor analysis of the EBLPS produced six factors — rather than the original twelve practice categories — tells us something important: governance practice, as boards actually experience and enact it, clusters in ways that are not perfectly predicted by how researchers initially categorize it. The six-factor structure is therefore itself a finding about the nature of board leadership.

The Six Factors: What Governance Actually Looks Like in Practice

While the paper's primary purpose is validation rather than exhaustive description of each factor, the six-factor structure reflects a meaningful organization of board practice. The analysis consolidated twelve distinct practices into six underlying dimensions, suggesting that many of the practices researchers identify as separate are functionally interdependent — boards that do one tend to do the others in the same cluster. For practitioners, this means governance improvement is not a matter of independently checking off a dozen behaviors, but of developing the orientations and habits from which related practices flow.

The reduction from twelve to six also has practical implications for assessment and improvement work. A governance diagnostic built on six coherent dimensions is more actionable than one built on twelve undifferentiated practices — it provides leverage points where change in one practice is likely to ripple through related behaviors in the same cluster.

Achievement Group Differences: The Core Empirical Claim

The t-test comparisons between higher- and lower-achieving districts are the study's most practically significant finding. That measurable, statistically significant score differences exist between these groups supports the construct validity of the instrument — it is measuring something that tracks with real-world educational outcomes. More substantively, it reinforces a body of evidence suggesting that governance practices are not merely procedural overhead but are systematically associated with the educational environment districts create.

It is worth being precise about what this finding does and does not establish. T-test comparisons between achievement groups confirm association; they do not establish that better governance causes higher achievement. Districts that achieve at higher levels may differ in many ways — funding, demographics, community characteristics, superintendent quality — and disentangling governance practices from these confounds requires research designs this study does not employ. The finding is nonetheless meaningful: governance quality varies in ways that track student outcomes, and that variation is captured by the EBLPS instrument.

Implications for Governance Practice

For school board practitioners, governance improvement programs, and state agencies designing oversight frameworks, the EBLPS offers a concrete tool for moving governance assessment from impressionistic to systematic. The instrument's grounding in a literature review of achievement-associated practices means it is not measuring generic "good governance" but specifically the governance behaviors that research connects to student outcomes. Used well, it shifts governance evaluation from compliance monitoring to learning-oriented inquiry.

  • Diagnostic use: Boards can use the EBLPS to identify which of the six governance dimensions are strengths and which need development, rather than treating governance quality as a single undifferentiated attribute.
  • Longitudinal tracking: Because the instrument is validated, it can measure change over time — boards can assess whether deliberate governance improvement efforts actually shift their scores on the relevant dimensions.
  • Comparison across districts: The instrument creates a common metric that allows districts, improvement programs, and state agencies to make meaningful comparisons rather than relying on qualitative impressions.
  • Training design: The six-factor structure can inform the design of board professional development — training organized around the actual dimensions of governance practice is more coherent than training that treats each practice as an independent item on a checklist.
The Bottom Line

Johnson's study provides the governance field with a validated instrument for measuring the board leadership practices most associated with student achievement. The six-factor structure and the measurable achievement-group differences establish that these practices form coherent, assessable dimensions — not just a list of aspirational behaviors. Districts and improvement programs that want to move governance assessment from anecdote to evidence have in the EBLPS a research-grounded starting point.
