Core Finding: Board members' stated understanding of federal and state accountability requirements did not match how they actually used assessment data. Rather than following prescribed data-use frameworks, boards synthesized external policy guidance into locally adapted approaches — demonstrating that local governance actors are active interpreters of policy, not passive implementers. This dynamic has grown more consequential as ESSA shifted greater accountability discretion to the state and district level.
Methodology: Qualitative case study design examining how school board members made sense of federal accountability mandates and subsequently used assessment data. Sensemaking theory grounded the analysis, treating board members as meaning-makers who interpret policy through the lens of their own mental models and local context. Data collection included interviews and document review; the design prioritizes depth of understanding over generalizability.
For Practitioners: Boards that believe they are following accountability policy may be unknowingly diverging from it in ways that matter for district strategy and compliance. This study is a prompt for boards to audit the gap between their stated understanding of accountability frameworks and their actual data review and goal-setting practices — and to build explicit processes for translating policy requirements into governance decisions rather than relying on informal interpretation.

Sensemaking as the Central Framework

Sutherland's study is grounded in sensemaking theory — the idea that people do not simply receive and execute information but actively construct meaning from it, especially in conditions of complexity and ambiguity. Federal and state accountability policy is precisely that kind of environment: layered mandates, shifting requirements, technical assessment language, and implicit expectations about how data should inform decision-making. For school board members, who are largely non-experts operating in a high-stakes policy domain, the gap between what policy says and what board members believe it says is structurally predictable.

The study reveals that this gap is not merely a comprehension problem. Board members developed their own mental models of what accountability meant, filtered through local context, community values, and their own interpretive frameworks. When they used assessment data, they were not failing to implement policy — they were implementing their version of it, which diverged from the federal and state versions in ways they may not have recognized. This is a meaningful distinction: it shifts the analysis from individual deficit (boards that don't understand policy) toward systemic dynamics (policy that does not reliably transfer intent to local actors).

The Disconnect Between Understanding and Practice

One of the study's most practitioner-relevant findings is the observable disconnect between how board members talked about accountability mandates and how they actually engaged with assessment data. Board members could describe accountability requirements in general terms, but when data review entered the governance process, their approach diverged from both federal guidance and state directives. They were not simply ignorant of the requirements — they had constructed interpretations that felt consistent with their understanding while differing in practice from what policymakers intended.

This pattern has direct implications for governance design. Training board members on what accountability policy requires is a necessary but insufficient intervention. If board members filter that training through existing interpretive frameworks and then construct locally-adapted practices, surface-level knowledge transfer does not change governance behavior. What changes behavior is structured processes: explicit protocols for how assessment data enters board deliberation, who frames it, and what questions the board is expected to answer with it. Without those structures, sensemaking fills the gap — and the resulting practices may bear little resemblance to policy intent.

Local Adaptation as a Feature and a Risk

The study is careful not to frame local divergence purely as a governance failure. Board members' capacity to synthesize external policy guidance into locally customized strategies can be understood as democratic responsiveness — communities governing themselves in ways that reflect local priorities rather than mechanically executing federal mandates. Under ESSA, which deliberately shifted accountability discretion downward to states and districts, this local adaptation is arguably what the policy architecture intended.

But the same dynamic carries real risks. If board members' mental models of accountability are poorly calibrated — shaped more by political pressure, community preferences, or misread policy language than by accurate understanding of what outcomes-focused accountability requires — their locally adapted practices may systematically underserve students, especially those in subgroups that accountability policy was specifically designed to protect. The question is not whether local adaptation happens (it always does), but whether boards have the knowledge infrastructure and governance processes to make that adaptation defensible rather than accidental.

Implications for Governance Practice

Sutherland's findings point toward a specific set of governance design interventions. The problem is not that board members lack good intentions — it is that the path from accountability policy to board practice runs through informal interpretation rather than structured protocol. Closing that gap requires deliberate design at the governance level.

  • Audit the understanding-practice gap: Boards should periodically examine not just what members say they understand about accountability requirements, but how those requirements are actually operationalized in data review cycles, goal-setting, and superintendent evaluation. The gap between stated understanding and actual practice is the diagnostic target.
  • Build structured data protocols: Unstructured data review invites sensemaking divergence. Boards that use explicit protocols — specifying which data sources are reviewed, in what sequence, with what questions — reduce the interpretive variance that produces drift from accountability intent.
  • Name the local adaptation explicitly: When boards make locally-informed decisions that deviate from state or federal guidance, that deviation should be conscious and documented rather than inadvertent. Intentional local adaptation is defensible; unrecognized drift is not.
  • Connect policy changes to governance process review: ESSA's shift toward state-level accountability systems changed what boards were responsible for interpreting. Boards operating under mental models calibrated to NCLB-era requirements are likely to have larger sensemaking gaps than those that have explicitly updated their frameworks for the ESSA context.
The Bottom Line

Boards do not implement accountability policy — they interpret it, and their interpretations diverge from both federal and state intent in ways they may not recognize. Sutherland's study is a reminder that governance behavior is shaped as much by mental models and local context as by policy requirements, and that knowledge transfer alone is insufficient to align board practice with accountability frameworks. The design implication is structural: boards need explicit data review protocols, deliberate self-assessment of their interpretive assumptions, and regular audits of the gap between what they believe their accountability obligations are and how they actually use assessment data in governance decisions.
