Core Finding: Using survey data from Minnesota school board members, Ford and Ihrke found that charter school board members are significantly more likely to define accountability in terms of high-stakes testing outcomes, while traditional elected board members more often define accountability through staff development and administrative oversight frameworks. The divergence reflects not just preference differences but structurally different theories of governance embedded in the two board types.
Methodology: The study drew on survey data from both democratically elected traditional public school board members and nonprofit charter school board members in Minnesota. Board members were asked to define accountability in their governance context. Responses were compared across board type using statistical analysis to identify systematic differences in how the two populations conceptualize their oversight role.
For Practitioners: The research is a direct warning about accountability drift: when governance is moved from elected bodies to appointed nonprofit boards, the accountability framework boards use to evaluate themselves and their schools changes in ways that may not align with what families and communities expect. Practitioners designing governance systems should treat "accountability" as a contested term, not a shared assumption, and surface definitional differences explicitly before designing oversight structures.

What This Study Examined

The expansion of charter schooling in the United States has transferred educational governance authority from democratically elected school boards to appointed nonprofit boards in a significant and largely underexamined way. While policy debates about charter schools often center on student outcomes, curriculum, and resource allocation, Ford and Ihrke's 2017 study asked a more foundational question: do the people sitting on charter boards and traditional public school boards actually mean the same thing when they use the word "accountability"? The study's answer is a clear no — and the differences matter.

Minnesota provided a useful research setting because it has both a long history of charter schooling and a functioning traditional public school governance structure, giving the researchers access to meaningful samples of both board types. By surveying board members directly and asking them to define accountability in their own governance context, the study accessed something more revealing than behavioral data alone: it captured the cognitive frameworks board members bring to their oversight role before any particular decision is made.

Two Different Definitions of the Same Word

Charter school board members in the sample were significantly more likely to frame accountability in terms of high-stakes testing — student performance data as the primary lens through which the school's success or failure is judged. This is an output-focused accountability model: what matters is whether students produce measurable results on standardized assessments, and the board's role is to monitor and respond to those results. The logic is clean and quantifiable, which explains its appeal in the nonprofit governance context, where boards often come from business or philanthropic backgrounds accustomed to outcome metrics.

Traditional public school board members, by contrast, more frequently defined accountability through staff development and administrative frameworks — an input-and-process orientation that emphasizes building organizational capacity, supporting the superintendent and instructional staff, and maintaining sound administrative systems. This is not an anti-outcomes view; it reflects a different theory of how boards drive results: by investing in people and processes rather than primarily monitoring test scores. It also reflects the democratic accountability layer that elected boards carry — they answer to voters for the quality of the entire educational enterprise, not just its most measurable outputs.

Why the Governance Structure Shapes the Accountability Frame

The definitional differences Ford and Ihrke document are not random. They are structurally produced by the different accountability environments the two board types inhabit. Elected school board members are embedded in a democratic accountability chain: they face voters, they are visible community figures, and their legitimacy derives from representational authority. This creates incentives to think broadly about accountability — to parents, to staff, to community members who are not parents of school-age children but pay taxes and hold civic expectations. High-stakes testing is one accountability signal among many they must integrate.

Charter board members, by contrast, serve on nonprofit boards that are appointed rather than elected. Their formal accountability runs upward to the authorizer and the organization's mission, not laterally to a voting public. This changes which signals are salient. Authorizers typically evaluate charter schools through performance frameworks anchored in test score data. Board members internalize these frameworks as the definition of what accountability means in their context. The study suggests that the shift from elected to appointed governance does not just change who sits in the boardroom — it changes the cognitive architecture of oversight.

Implications for Governance Practice

The findings carry direct implications for anyone designing, evaluating, or participating in school board governance. The core implication is that accountability is not a neutral concept with a self-evident meaning — it is a framework that different actors construct differently based on their structural position, accountability relationships, and theories of change. When governance systems assume a shared definition without surfacing the underlying framework, accountability conversations quickly become talking past each other.

  • Make accountability definitions explicit: Before any board designs an accountability framework for its superintendent or school, members should articulate what accountability means to them. The Ford and Ihrke findings suggest those definitions will vary — and unexamined variation produces incoherent oversight.
  • Recognize democratic accountability as distinct: Elected boards carry an accountability obligation that nonprofit boards do not — they answer to constituents who did not choose the board and cannot resign their stake in the public school system. This is not a defect; it is a structural feature that shapes which accountability signals boards are obligated to respond to.
  • Test score accountability is not comprehensive accountability: The charter boards' tendency to define accountability through high-stakes testing reflects a real metric — but a narrow one. Boards that reduce accountability to test performance systematically underweight inputs, processes, equity dimensions, and community trust signals that are also part of a complete governance picture.
  • Authorizer design has downstream accountability consequences: Charter authorizers who evaluate schools primarily through test score frameworks are shaping how charter boards define their own oversight role. Authorizers interested in broader accountability should examine whether their evaluation frameworks are producing the accountability orientation they intend.

The Democratic Accountability Gap

The study's most consequential contribution may be its illumination of what is lost — or at least transformed — when democratic accountability structures are replaced by nonprofit governance. The shift is not simply administrative. It changes who boards answer to, what signals they prioritize, and how they define success. For policymakers expanding charter authorization or designing hybrid governance models, these findings are a direct data point: governance structure shapes accountability culture, and accountability culture shapes what boards actually do. The research does not argue that high-stakes testing is an illegitimate accountability measure; it argues that an accountability framework built entirely on test scores, without the broader obligations that democratic governance creates, is a narrower thing than what public education has historically required of its governing bodies.

The Bottom Line

Ford and Ihrke document a fundamental divergence in how charter and traditional public school board members define accountability — charter boards anchor to high-stakes testing outcomes, while traditional boards emphasize staff development and administrative frameworks. This is not merely a philosophical difference: it shapes what oversight looks like in practice, whose interests boards are structured to serve, and whether governance arrangements produce the accountability relationships communities actually need. Any serious effort to improve school board governance must treat "accountability" as a term to be defined, not assumed.
