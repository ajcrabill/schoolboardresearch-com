Core Finding: Citizen participation at school board meetings is a recognizable speech genre with its own conventions and constraints. When a community is in conflict, speakers rely on six recurring negative-sentiment strategies to express opposition: feeling-limned description, avowal of feelings, rhetorical questions, reported speech, use of god and devil terms, and using meeting rules as weapons. The authors argue that "reasonable hostility" — structured, pointed, but bounded — is a legitimate and even desirable form of democratic expression at public meetings.
Methodology: Qualitative discourse analysis of citizen commentary from school board meetings in a single US community experiencing active governance conflict. The study draws on recorded meeting speech to characterize the structural features of the citizen commentary genre and to catalog and analyze the rhetorical strategies speakers use to express negative sentiment. This is not a survey or experimental study — it is an in-depth linguistic and communicative analysis of naturally occurring civic speech.
For Practitioners: Board members and administrators who understand how citizen commentary works as a genre — and who can recognize negative-sentiment strategies as legitimate communicative acts rather than mere disruption — are better positioned to chair meetings fairly, respond with appropriate gravity, and preserve community trust even when the room is hostile. Procedural management that suppresses "reasonable hostility" may reduce conflict in the short term while deepening civic alienation over time.

Citizen Commentary as a Speech Genre

Tracy and Durfy open with a premise that is easy to overlook: the three-minute public comment at a school board meeting is not just a democratic formality. It is a speech genre — a recognizable, structured form of communication with its own conventions about who speaks, in what order, for how long, on what topics, and with what kinds of moves considered appropriate or transgressive. Like other speech genres (the courtroom testimony, the job interview, the academic lecture), citizen commentary at board meetings has evolved under specific institutional pressures, and those pressures shape what speakers can and cannot say effectively.

The study is grounded in a single US school district experiencing significant community conflict — a context the authors treat as analytically useful precisely because conflict makes the genre's constraints visible. When speakers are angry, at odds with one another, and skeptical of board legitimacy, the rhetorical strategies they reach for reveal what the genre permits, what it punishes, and what it demands. A study of routine, low-conflict public comment might find only politeness norms; this study, conducted in a community in crisis, finds the full expressive range of the genre.

Six Negative-Sentiment Strategies

The central empirical contribution of the article is a taxonomy of the discourse practices citizens use to express negative sentiment — opposition, frustration, distrust, or anger toward the board or toward one another — within the constraints of the public comment format. Six strategies emerge from the analysis with enough frequency and consistency to constitute named patterns.

Feeling-limned description embeds emotional content in seemingly factual accounts: rather than stating "I am angry," speakers narrate an event in language saturated with affective meaning, allowing the feeling to accumulate through detail. Avowal of feelings is the more direct version: explicit first-person declaration of emotional state, used to claim authenticity and moral seriousness. Rhetorical questions perform challenge without making falsifiable assertions — they imply that the answer is obvious and that the board has failed to provide it. Reported speech imports the voices of others (other community members, past board statements, news coverage) into the speaker's commentary, creating the effect of collective witness rather than individual complaint.

The final two strategies operate at the boundary of the genre itself. God and devil terms — Kenneth Burke's concept, adapted here — are the evaluatively charged vocabulary of civic life: words like "transparency," "children," and "accountability" carry strong positive freight, while "politics," "cover-up," and "rubber stamp" carry negative freight. Speakers invoke these terms to frame the board's behavior in morally unambiguous ways that are difficult to rebut within the genre's constraints. Most striking is the sixth strategy: using meeting rules as weapons. When speakers invoke procedural rules — time limits, agenda constraints, speaking order — not to facilitate orderly discussion but to challenge the board's own procedural conduct, they turn the governance apparatus against itself. A speaker who points out that the board chair cut off a community member 20 seconds early while letting a board-friendly speaker run long is using the meeting's own legitimating rules to delegitimate the board.

The Case for Reasonable Hostility

Tracy and Durfy conclude with an argument that is likely to unsettle some practitioners: they contend that "reasonable hostility" — civic expression that is pointed, adversarial, and emotionally charged, but that operates within recognizable communicative norms — is not a problem to be managed but a feature of democratic life to be preserved. This is a direct challenge to the discourse of "civil discourse" that pervades board governance training, which often treats hostility as a failure of community health rather than a legitimate communicative act.

The authors distinguish reasonable hostility from unreasonable hostility not by its emotional intensity but by its structure. Reasonable hostility is targeted, traceable, and responsive to evidence; it operates within the genre's constraints even while straining against them. Unreasonable hostility — personal threats, harassment, bad-faith procedural abuse — falls outside the genre and outside democratic norms. Boards that conflate the two and treat all negative sentiment as a civility problem risk suppressing exactly the kind of critical citizen voice that democratic governance depends on. The three-minute public comment period exists, in part, so that people who distrust the board can say so — and boards that manage this away are managing away a legitimate accountability mechanism.

What the Genre Demands of Boards

Understanding citizen commentary as a speech genre also reframes what is demanded of board members and meeting chairs. Speakers in this genre are operating under significant constraints: limited time, no right of reply, no guarantee of board acknowledgment, and the knowledge that their words are almost certainly not going to change that night's vote. The negative-sentiment strategies Tracy and Durfy identify are in part adaptations to these constraints — ways of making an impact within a format that structurally limits impact. A board chair who understands this is less likely to interpret hostility as mere dysfunction and more likely to recognize it as a communicative response to a difficult communicative situation.

The study also has implications for how boards design and run public comment processes. If the genre's constraints predictably produce certain rhetorical moves — and if those moves are legitimate democratic expression — then design choices that further constrict the genre (shorter time limits, narrower topic scopes, stricter civility enforcement) may not reduce civic conflict so much as compress it. The underlying discontent remains; the outlet for it shrinks. Conversely, design choices that expand the genre's expressive space — longer time limits for contested agenda items, structured opportunities for speakers to respond to board explanations, acknowledgment of public comment during deliberation — may reduce the pressure that produces the most adversarial rhetorical moves.

Implications for Governance Practice

This study is not a quantitative evaluation of board performance or student outcomes. Its value for practitioners lies in its analytical framework: a way of seeing and interpreting public comment that moves beyond "was the meeting civil?" toward "what communicative work were speakers doing, and what were the structural conditions that shaped it?" Boards and superintendents who develop this kind of genre literacy are better equipped to run public meetings as genuine democratic venues rather than compliance rituals.

  • Recognize the genre's constraints: Speakers in public comment have limited time, no reply right, and no assurance of being heard. Adversarial rhetorical strategies are often rational responses to these constraints, not evidence of bad faith.
  • Distinguish reasonable from unreasonable hostility: Pointed, emotionally charged criticism of board decisions or conduct is reasonable hostility and is a legitimate form of democratic expression. Personal threats, harassment, and sustained bad-faith disruption are not. Train chairs to distinguish between them rather than treating all hostility uniformly.
  • Monitor your own procedural conduct: The "using meeting rules as weapons" strategy becomes available to speakers when boards themselves are inconsistent in procedural enforcement. Fair, visible, consistent application of time limits and speaking rules removes the most potent procedural weapon from adversarial speakers.
  • Treat god and devil terms as diagnostic signals: When speakers invoke accountability, transparency, or cover-up, they are flagging that the board's conduct has triggered a legitimacy concern. The specific vocabulary is less important than the underlying signal — something in the board's behavior has activated a trust deficit worth examining.
  • Acknowledge negative sentiment without endorsing its claims: Boards that respond to hostile public comment by visibly ignoring it, shutting it down prematurely, or responding only to friendly speakers validate the speaker's implicit claim that the board is not listening. A brief, non-defensive acknowledgment — "I hear that you have strong concerns about this decision" — does not concede the argument and may significantly reduce the communicative pressure in the room.
The Bottom Line

Tracy and Durfy provide school governance practitioners with something most board training does not: a rigorous analytical account of what is actually happening communicatively during contentious public comment, and why. The six negative-sentiment strategies they identify are not failures of civic culture — they are coherent rhetorical adaptations to the structural constraints of a genre that limits speakers' power. Boards that understand this are better positioned to chair meetings fairly, respond to hostility without being destabilized by it, and preserve the democratic integrity of a process that is, at its best, a genuine accountability mechanism.
