Core Finding: Superintendents whose self-assessments of their accountability behaviors closely aligned with how their central office administrators rated them led districts with substantially higher student achievement in both mathematics and reading. Agreement between superintendent self-perception and subordinate perception — not simply the superintendent's own accountability behaviors — was the key predictor, explaining 22% of math score variance and 19% of reading score variance after controlling for socioeconomic factors.
Methodology: Quantitative study using hierarchical multiple regression and covariance analyses. Superintendents completed self-assessments of their accountability behaviors while central office administrators independently rated the same superintendent behaviors. The agreement between these two rating perspectives was calculated as the key independent variable. Student achievement outcomes in mathematics and reading served as dependent variables, with socioeconomic controls applied throughout.
For Practitioners: School boards should treat superintendent-central office alignment as a governance indicator, not merely a management concern. If a superintendent's self-perception of their accountability practices diverges substantially from how cabinet members and central office leaders experience those same practices, this study suggests district outcomes will suffer. Boards that evaluate superintendents in isolation — without gathering 360-degree data from the central office team — are missing one of the strongest predictors of achievement identified in this research.

The Alignment Problem at the Top of the Organization

A longstanding assumption in educational leadership is that superintendent effectiveness flows primarily from the quality of the superintendent's own behaviors — how well they set direction, allocate resources, build culture, and communicate expectations. Hough's 2014 study complicates this picture in a practically important way. The variable that most powerfully predicted student achievement was not the absolute level of accountability behaviors the superintendent reported, nor the level subordinates reported. It was the agreement between those two perspectives. Districts where superintendents and their central office administrators held convergent views of the superintendent's accountability practices significantly outperformed districts where those views diverged.

This finding shifts the analysis from individual leadership quality to organizational alignment. A superintendent who believes she is consistently holding staff accountable for outcomes, while her central office team experiences a different reality, is operating in a district where the leadership system is not coherent. That incoherence — not any specific behavioral deficit — appears to be the mechanism through which student outcomes are suppressed.

What Internal Accountability Means in This Context

Hough operationalizes superintendent accountability behaviors to include the actions superintendents take to establish clear performance expectations, monitor progress toward those expectations, and respond to performance data throughout the organization. These are the behaviors that translate district-level goals into organizational pressure and support at the school and classroom level. When a superintendent reports engaging in these behaviors at high levels, but central office administrators experience something different, the district faces a version of the knowing-doing gap that organizational research has documented across sectors: the leader believes the strategy is being executed while the organization is receiving a different signal.

The self-other agreement variable captures exactly this gap. It is calculated as the degree of convergence between the superintendent's self-rating and the mean rating from central office subordinates on the same accountability behavior items. High agreement — whether at a high or low absolute level — indicates that the superintendent and their senior team share a common understanding of what accountability looks like in their district. The study's central finding is that this shared understanding, this organizational coherence around accountability norms, is what drives outcomes.

Effect Size and Practical Significance

The magnitude of the findings warrants attention. In a field where individual variables rarely explain more than a few percentage points of outcome variance after socioeconomic controls, the self-other agreement variable accounting for 22% of mathematics score variance and 19% of reading score variance is a substantial result. Hierarchical regression analysis allowed the researchers to isolate this contribution: socioeconomic controls were entered in earlier blocks, meaning the agreement variable's explanatory power reflects something beyond the well-documented relationship between poverty and achievement. Superintendent-central office alignment predicts student performance over and above what family income levels alone would predict.

This effect size is comparable to findings from the strongest governance and leadership research in the field and suggests that internal organizational coherence around accountability is not a soft or peripheral factor. It is a structural condition with detectable consequences for the children districts serve.

Implications for Superintendent Evaluation

Most school board superintendent evaluation processes rely exclusively on the superintendent's own self-assessment, supplemented by the board's direct observations of the superintendent's behavior. Central office staff — the people positioned closest to the execution of the superintendent's accountability practices — are rarely included as formal sources of evaluative data. Hough's findings offer a direct challenge to this design choice.

If the gap between superintendent self-perception and central office perception is one of the strongest predictors of student achievement available in the literature, then evaluation systems that do not measure this gap are systematically omitting a high-signal indicator. Boards that add structured 360-degree feedback from cabinet and central office administrators to their evaluation processes are not simply gathering more data — they are capturing the specific alignment dynamic that this research identifies as consequential.

Implications for Governance Practice

The governance implications of this study extend beyond evaluation instrument design to the board's broader role in monitoring organizational health. A board that receives only the superintendent's account of how accountability is functioning in the district has a structurally incomplete picture. This study provides empirical grounding for boards to seek regular, structured feedback from senior staff as part of their oversight function — not as a substitute for trusting the superintendent, but as a way of ensuring that the organizational reality matches the superintendent's representation of it.

  • 360-degree evaluation design: Boards should incorporate structured central office feedback into superintendent evaluation. Hough's findings suggest this is not a nice-to-have feature but a high-leverage design choice with direct links to student outcomes.
  • Alignment as a monitoring indicator: When a superintendent reports high accountability activity but central office administrators describe something different, that divergence is a governance warning sign. Boards should treat it as information requiring follow-up, not as a personality conflict to manage around.
  • Onboarding and norming conversations: New superintendent-board relationships should include structured conversations about what accountability looks like in practice — what it means to hold a principal accountable, how central office is expected to support and monitor school performance. These conversations reduce the conditions that produce self-other rating divergence.
  • Socioeconomic framing: Because the study controlled for socioeconomic factors, boards in high-poverty districts cannot attribute low achievement to demographics alone. The alignment variable retains explanatory power across economic contexts, suggesting that organizational coherence is a lever available to all districts regardless of the challenges they face.
The Bottom Line

Hough's research identifies a specific, measurable organizational condition — the degree to which superintendents and central office administrators share a common perception of the superintendent's accountability behaviors — as one of the most powerful predictors of district student achievement identified in the governance literature. A superintendent who accurately perceives how their accountability practices are experienced by their senior team leads a more coherent organization, and that coherence has direct consequences for student outcomes. Boards that want to take this research seriously should build 360-degree feedback from central office administrators into their evaluation systems and treat significant self-other rating divergence as an organizational health indicator requiring attention.
