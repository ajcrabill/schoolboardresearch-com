Core Finding: School board presidents who perceived their superintendent as communicatively competent — specifically empathetic, attentive, willing to self-disclose, free from social anxiety, and versatile in style — were significantly more likely to report that board members supported the superintendent's agenda items. The study found a statistically significant relationship between overall interpersonal communication competence and board voting support, particularly for agenda items with immediate consequences for the school system.
Methodology: Exploratory survey study grounded in interpersonal communication theory. Researchers surveyed school board presidents to capture their perceptions of their superintendent's communication behaviors across five dimensions — empathy, listening, self-disclosure, social anxiety (absence thereof), and versatility — and examined covariance between those perceptions and board voting decisions. Statistical analysis identified significant relationships between the composite communication competence measure and board support on agenda items.
For Practitioners: Superintendents who invest in developing interpersonal communication skills — not just policy expertise or technical competence — are likely to find boards more responsive to their recommendations. Boards evaluating superintendent performance should consider communication quality as a substantive governance variable, not a peripheral soft skill.

The Communication Premise Behind Board-Superintendent Dynamics

The relationship between a superintendent and the school board is one of the most consequential and least formally structured in public education governance. The superintendent serves simultaneously as the board's chief executive officer, its primary policy advisor, and the central conduit through which the board understands what is happening in the district. That role is inherently communicative — not just transactional. Petersen and Short's study draws on interpersonal communication theory to examine whether the quality of that communication, as perceived by board presidents, shapes the board's actual decision-making behavior. The answer, the data suggest, is yes.

The theoretical grounding here matters. Interpersonal communication research identifies several components of communication competence — empathy, listening, self-disclosure, social anxiety, and versatility — as particularly potent elements of persuasion and relational influence. The study applies that framework to the board-superintendent relationship, treating the superintendent not merely as an information provider but as an interpersonal actor whose communication style either builds or erodes board confidence and responsiveness. This is a different lens than most superintendent evaluation frameworks, which tend to focus on management outcomes, strategic planning, or curriculum leadership rather than the quality of interpersonal exchange.

What the Five Dimensions Measure

The five communication competence dimensions examined in the study each capture a distinct aspect of how a superintendent engages with board members. Empathy refers to the superintendent's demonstrated understanding of the board's perspectives, concerns, and values — the degree to which board members feel heard and understood rather than managed. Listening captures whether the superintendent processes and responds to what board members actually say rather than simply waiting to deliver prepared information. Self-disclosure reflects the superintendent's willingness to share relevant information about their own reasoning, uncertainty, or decision-making process — transparency in the relational sense, not merely the procedural one.

The absence of social anxiety is perhaps the least intuitive dimension but carries real practical weight. Superintendents who communicate with confidence and ease in board settings — who do not appear to avoid, deflect, or become defensive under questioning — are more credible and more persuasive. Versatility, the fifth dimension, measures adaptive capacity: the superintendent's ability to adjust communication style to different board members, different issues, and different relational dynamics rather than applying a single default mode regardless of context. Together, these five dimensions describe a superintendent who is not just technically informed but relationally attuned.

Board Voting and the Communication Connection

The study's central empirical finding is a statistically significant relationship between overall interpersonal communication competence and board support on agenda items — particularly items with immediate consequences for the school system. This is not a minor finding. It suggests that a superintendent's ability to secure board approval for consequential decisions is meaningfully tied to how board presidents perceive the quality of the superintendent's interpersonal engagement, not only to the merits of the proposals themselves.

This has implications for how we think about board-superintendent alignment. When boards and superintendents appear to be in conflict — when votes are close, when agenda items stall, when board members consistently push back or defer — communication breakdown is a plausible contributing factor. The tendency in governance consulting is to attribute these patterns to role confusion (boards micromanaging), political conflict (board factions), or policy disagreement. Petersen and Short's work suggests another variable deserves attention: whether the superintendent has developed the interpersonal communication competencies that generate trust and receptivity in the board's most influential members.

Implications for Governance Practice

  • Communication competence belongs in superintendent evaluation: If board decision-making is demonstrably linked to how well superintendents communicate, then communication quality is a legitimate and important dimension of superintendent performance — not a background personality variable to be noted but not scored.
  • Empathy and listening are strategic, not just relational: Superintendents who cultivate genuine attentiveness to board members' perspectives — who practice active listening and reflect back understanding rather than rushing to persuade — are building the relational capital that makes their recommendations land. This is governance strategy, not pleasantness for its own sake.
  • Self-disclosure reduces board anxiety: Boards that know what the superintendent is thinking, why they are recommending what they are recommending, and where the superintendent is uncertain are less likely to feel managed or surprised. Transparent interpersonal communication decreases the defensive vigilance that produces split votes and lengthy deliberations.
  • Versatility is a trainable competency: The ability to adapt communication style to different board members is not fixed temperament — it can be developed through coaching, practice, and reflection. Superintendent preparation programs and professional development providers should treat communication versatility as a core skill, not a supplementary module.
  • Board presidents are a high-leverage audience: This study focuses specifically on board presidents, who play an outsized role in setting agendas, framing deliberations, and shaping how other board members understand issues. Superintendents who invest disproportionate communication effort in maintaining strong relationships with board presidents are allocating attention strategically.

Limitations and Contextual Notes

This is an exploratory study conducted in 2002, and its methodological design has constraints that practitioners should keep in mind. The data reflect board presidents' perceptions of their superintendent's communication — not independent observation, not board member ratings, and not the superintendent's self-assessment. Perception-based measures are appropriate for studying relational influence (the board's willingness to vote yes is itself a perceptual and social phenomenon), but they cannot establish whether superintendents who are perceived as communicatively competent actually are, or whether communication competence drives board support versus some unmeasured third variable driving both.

The study is also two decades old, and the governance context has shifted. Board composition has become more politically contentious in many districts; social media has changed how board members form and express opinions before meetings; superintendent tenure has shortened; and the issues reaching board votes have become more polarized in many communities. Whether the specific communication dimensions identified in 2002 carry the same weight under current conditions is an open empirical question. That said, the core theoretical proposition — that interpersonal communication quality shapes relational trust, and relational trust shapes decision-making support — is robust enough to remain relevant even as the surface context changes.

The Bottom Line

Petersen and Short make a practically important point in empirically tractable form: school board decision-making is not purely a function of policy merits or political alignment. It is shaped, in measurable ways, by how well the superintendent communicates with the board's most influential members. Empathy, active listening, transparency, confident engagement, and adaptive style are not soft skills decorating technical competence — they are substantive governance variables. Superintendents who develop these capacities, and boards that evaluate them, are likely to function with greater coherence and decisiveness than those who treat communication as incidental to the work.
