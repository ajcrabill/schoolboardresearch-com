Core Finding: Factor analysis of a 33-item board leadership survey revealed six underlying dimensions of effective board practice. School districts scoring significantly higher on these six factors also demonstrated significantly higher student achievement, providing empirical support for the connection between board governance behaviors and educational outcomes.
Methodology: Instrument development study combining literature-based item generation, expert review, factor analysis, and t-test comparison. Researchers identified 12 board leadership practices from prior literature, built a 33-item survey (the Effective Board Leadership Practices Survey, or EBLPS), and administered it across school districts classified as high- or low-achieving based on state accountability data. Factor analysis was used to establish construct validity; t-tests assessed whether high- and low-achieving districts produced significantly different EBLPS scores.
For Practitioners: The EBLPS and its six factors give boards and their development partners a structured, research-grounded framework for identifying which governance behaviors most closely correspond to student achievement. Districts can use the instrument or its underlying factors for board self-assessment, professional development targeting, or evaluating governance improvement over time.

Why Instrument Validation Matters for Governance Research

Much of what is written about effective school board governance is either purely normative — here is what boards should do — or anecdotal, derived from case studies and practitioner interviews. Rigorous empirical measurement is comparatively rare. Johnson's study belongs to a less common category: it attempts to operationalize governance quality in a psychometrically defensible instrument and then test whether that instrument actually distinguishes boards in high-performing districts from those in low-performing ones. That design logic is important. A survey that merely captures what respondents believe good governance looks like, without validating that those beliefs predict outcomes, has limited practical value. The EBLPS was built to close that gap.

The development process followed standard instrument construction practice. Researchers began with a literature review that identified 12 board leadership practices linked, in prior work, to positive student outcomes. They wrote items to represent each practice, reviewed items with experts, and then submitted the 33-item instrument to factor analysis on a sample of board members and administrators. Factor analysis is appropriate here because it reveals the underlying structure of a set of items — whether respondents perceive 12 distinct practices or whether those practices cluster into a smaller number of coherent dimensions. The finding that six factors emerged (rather than 12 or 33) is itself substantively informative: some of the practices identified in the literature are not, in practice, perceived as distinct by those working within school governance systems.

The Six Factors: What They Tell Us

While the study does not fully enumerate or label each factor in the abstract, factor analysis in board governance research typically surfaces clusters around practices such as strategic focus and goal-setting, superintendent accountability and evaluation, data monitoring and use, community engagement and trust-building, board member role clarity and self-governance, and policy coherence. The fact that Johnson's analysis reduced 12 hypothesized practices to six interpretable factors suggests meaningful overlap among some of the original constructs — and invites practitioners to think not about a checklist of 12 separate behaviors but about a smaller number of more integrated governance competencies.

More important than the specific factor labels is the validation logic: the study tested whether boards in districts classified as high-achieving on state accountability measures scored significantly differently on these six factors than boards in low-achieving districts. They did. This is not proof of causation — districts that achieve more may also attract or develop stronger board leadership, and the instrument cannot distinguish direction of influence. But the association is meaningful and adds to a body of evidence suggesting that governance practice is not orthogonal to educational outcomes. What boards do appears to matter.

From Validation to Practice: Using the EBLPS Framework

The immediate practical application of this research is the instrument itself. Boards seeking an empirically validated self-assessment tool have relatively few options grounded in outcome-linked validation rather than expert consensus alone. The EBLPS was designed to fill that gap. A board that administers it — or uses its six underlying factors as a self-reflection framework — is working with a structure that has been tested against actual achievement differences, not merely derived from theoretical ideals.

There are important caveats for practitioners drawing on this work. The study was conducted in a specific state context with a particular sample of districts, and the achievement classification used to validate the instrument was based on the accountability metrics in use at the time of the research (2013). Boards applying this framework today should be attentive to whether the constructs still map onto their current context — particularly given the significant shifts in state accountability systems, board composition norms, and superintendent evaluation expectations since that period. The instrument's factor structure provides a durable conceptual scaffold even if specific items would benefit from updating.

Implications for Governance Practice

This study supports several practical conclusions for boards and the administrators who work with them. First, governance improvement efforts benefit from structured measurement — not just observation or anecdote. Second, effective board leadership appears to be multidimensional: single-factor frameworks (e.g., "boards just need to stay out of operations") likely miss the complexity of what distinguishes high-performing governance systems. Third, the six-factor structure suggests that board development programs should address governance in integrated clusters of practice rather than as an itemized list of discrete dos and don'ts.

  • Use validated instruments for board self-assessment: Self-evaluation tools grounded in empirical validation — not just expert opinion — are more likely to surface distinctions that actually predict outcomes.
  • Target development to underlying factor clusters: If board leadership organizes around six core dimensions, professional development should address those dimensions coherently rather than as a menu of isolated skills.
  • Treat instrument scores comparatively: The EBLPS was designed to distinguish high- from low-achieving districts, meaning its value lies partly in comparison — either against benchmarks or against a district's own prior scores over time.
  • Recognize the limits of cross-sectional association: Higher EBLPS scores in high-achieving districts could reflect better governance causing better outcomes, or better-resourced districts producing both. Professional development investments should be accompanied by longitudinal monitoring rather than assumed to be sufficient alone.
The Bottom Line

Johnson's study is one of the more methodologically careful attempts to connect specific board leadership behaviors to student achievement outcomes. By constructing and validating an instrument rather than relying on practitioner intuition, the research provides a structured, evidence-grounded foundation for board self-assessment. The six-factor structure — derived from how governance quality actually clusters in practice — offers a more nuanced map of effective board leadership than most development frameworks currently in use. Boards serious about governance quality should engage with this framework and consider how its dimensions map onto their own practice.
