Core Finding:Ninety percent of school boards are required to evaluate their superintendent, yet only 26% use the Professional Standards for Educational Leaders (PSEL 2015) as evaluation criteria. Compounding this gap, 90% of board members are not required to receive any training before conducting these evaluations, suggesting that most superintendent evaluations are both unmoored from professional standards and conducted by untrained evaluators.
Methodology:Survey study using a 35-question questionnaire administered to 76 school board members across two states, covering evaluation criteria, procedures, and professional development. Quantitative design with descriptive analysis of responses.
For Practitioners:Boards that evaluate superintendents without standards-aligned criteria or evaluator training risk subjective, inconsistent, and legally vulnerable processes. Adopting PSEL-aligned rubrics and requiring board member training before conducting evaluations are concrete, low-cost improvements most boards can implement immediately.

The Evaluation Mandate Without the Infrastructure

The core tension this study surfaces is straightforward: school boards are almost universally required to evaluate their superintendent, but the systems supporting that mandate are underdeveloped. Ninety percent of respondents reported their board was required to conduct superintendent evaluations — a figure that might suggest robust accountability infrastructure. The follow-on data undercuts that optimism. Only about one in four boards anchored their evaluation criteria to the Professional Standards for Educational Leaders, the nationally recognized framework developed specifically to define what excellent educational leadership looks like. The remaining boards are working from locally developed criteria, custom rubrics, or no clearly defined criteria at all — meaning that what gets measured, and how, varies enormously from district to district.

This variability matters because superintendent evaluation is not merely an HR exercise. It is the primary formal mechanism through which a board holds its chief executive accountable, communicates organizational priorities, and creates conditions for professional growth. Evaluations conducted without shared, standards-based criteria are less likely to focus on the leadership behaviors and outcomes most predictive of student success, and more likely to drift toward personal impressions, interpersonal dynamics, or compliance with narrow administrative tasks. When the person being evaluated has no clear picture of what excellent performance looks like, and the evaluators have no shared framework for assessing it, accountability becomes performance rather than substance.

The Training Gap Compounds the Criteria Gap

The study's second major finding is that board member training to conduct superintendent evaluations is nearly nonexistent as a requirement. Ninety percent of respondents reported they were not required to receive training before evaluating their superintendent. This is a structural problem, not a motivational one. Board members are community volunteers, not professional evaluators. Most arrive at their roles without backgrounds in educational leadership, personnel evaluation, or organizational assessment. Even well-intentioned board members conducting rigorous, good-faith evaluations are operating without the technical tools to do so effectively unless they have been trained.

The absence of training requirements interacts badly with the absence of standards-based criteria. A trained evaluator working from clear criteria can produce a defensible, useful assessment. An untrained evaluator working from vague criteria produces something that looks like accountability but functions more like an annual ritual. The study documents that evaluation methods also varied widely — written surveys, in-person meetings, goal-setting processes, and hybrid approaches all appeared in the data — suggesting there is no convergence even on procedural basics, let alone on substantive rigor.

  • Standards adoption is low: Only 26% of boards used PSEL 2015 as evaluation criteria, despite it being the field's primary professional framework for educational leadership.
  • Training is largely absent: 90% of board members face no training requirement before evaluating a superintendent, leaving most evaluators without formal preparation for a high-stakes task.
  • Procedural variation is wide: Evaluation methods varied significantly across districts, with no dominant standard approach — written, verbal, goal-based, and hybrid formats all in use.
  • Mandate-infrastructure mismatch: The near-universal requirement to evaluate is not matched by requirements for what to evaluate against or how to evaluate it, creating a compliance-without-substance dynamic.

Implications for Governance Practice

  • Adopt PSEL-aligned evaluation criteria: Boards using locally developed or informal criteria should review and revise their evaluation instruments against the Professional Standards for Educational Leaders. PSEL provides a publicly available, field-validated framework that makes the evaluation conversation more rigorous and less susceptible to personal preference or political dynamics.
  • Require evaluator training as a board norm: State associations, superintendents' associations, and state education agencies should establish training expectations — not just recommendations — for board members before they conduct superintendent evaluations. Even a single structured training session on evaluation criteria, goal-setting, and feedback delivery would meaningfully improve evaluation quality.
  • Standardize procedures without eliminating flexibility: While districts have legitimate reasons to adapt evaluation processes to local context, establishing a procedural floor — annual written evaluation, documented goals, structured feedback conversation — would reduce the risk of evaluations that are nominal rather than substantive.
  • Link evaluation to superintendent professional development: The study documents that professional development for superintendents is inconsistently connected to evaluation outcomes. Boards should treat evaluation findings as inputs to a development plan, not just a report card, aligning growth targets with the criteria used to assess performance.
The Bottom Line

This study confirms what governance reformers have long suspected: the formal requirement to evaluate a superintendent is not the same as the capacity to do it well. With only a quarter of boards using professional standards as criteria and almost none requiring evaluator training, most superintendent evaluations lack the infrastructure needed to function as genuine accountability mechanisms. Boards serious about superintendent accountability should treat standards adoption and board member training as foundational prerequisites, not optional enhancements.
