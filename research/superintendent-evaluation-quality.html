Core Finding: School board members recognize superintendent evaluation as their primary governance responsibility yet routinely fail to execute it well. Evaluation practices are inconsistent across and within boards, board members lack formal training in evaluation techniques, and the quality of the board-superintendent relationship measurably shapes how board members perceive and conduct the evaluation process — often undermining its rigor and utility for superintendent development.
Methodology: Qualitative study drawing on board member perspectives. The researcher examined three questions: what evaluation practices current boards use, what training and support board members say they need, and how the board-superintendent relationship influences evaluation perceptions. Data were gathered through participant interviews and analyzed for themes across the three inquiry areas.
For Practitioners: Boards should not assume that completing an annual evaluation form satisfies their oversight obligation. Evaluation quality requires structured processes, trained evaluators, and explicit attention to keeping the relational dynamics between board members and the superintendent from distorting what should be a professional accountability conversation. State associations and governance coaches have a concrete role to play in providing the training boards say they want but are not currently receiving.

The Gap Between Knowing and Doing

One of the more striking findings in Henrikson's study is not that boards ignore superintendent evaluation — it is that they recognize its importance while remaining systematically unable to do it well. Board members in this study understood that evaluating the superintendent is their primary governance responsibility. They were not unaware of the stakes. Yet awareness did not translate into competent practice. Evaluation processes were inconsistent, feedback was inadequately specific, and board members reported lacking the tools and training to conduct evaluations that would genuinely serve superintendent development.

This gap between acknowledged responsibility and actual practice is not unusual in governance contexts, but it is particularly consequential here. The superintendent is the board's sole direct report — the single employee whose performance most directly shapes district execution. When boards cannot evaluate that employee rigorously, they lose one of their most important levers for holding the organization accountable and supporting executive improvement. A nominal evaluation that produces no actionable feedback is not just a missed opportunity; it is a governance failure that compounds over years of a superintendent's tenure.

How Relationships Distort Evaluation

The study's third research question — about how the board-superintendent relationship influences evaluation — produced findings with significant practical implications. Board members' perceptions of and conduct during superintendent evaluations were shaped by the quality of their interpersonal relationships with the superintendent. Positive relationships created social pressure toward favorable assessments. Strained relationships risked producing evaluations inflected by conflict rather than evidence. In neither case was the evaluation primarily driven by performance data against established goals.

This relational distortion is a structural problem, not a personal failing. Boards are small bodies whose members interact frequently with the superintendent in both formal and informal contexts. Maintaining evaluative objectivity under those conditions requires deliberate process design — goal-setting that is documented and specific enough to anchor the evaluation, criteria established before the performance period rather than retrospectively, and norms that separate the professional accountability conversation from the ongoing working relationship. Without those structural supports, the relationship will tend to dominate the evaluation regardless of individual board member intentions.

Training Gaps and the Demand for Support

Board members in this study expressed a clear desire for ongoing professional development in evaluation techniques. This finding matters because it establishes that the training gap is not a supply problem that boards are passive about — boards want help and are not receiving it in adequate or consistent form. State school board associations, governance coaches, and professional development providers have a direct mandate here: the people responsible for one of governance's most consequential functions are asking for structured support in performing it.

The training need is also not generic. Superintendent evaluation requires specific competencies: how to set measurable goals that can anchor an annual evaluation, how to gather performance evidence beyond the superintendent's self-report, how to deliver feedback that is specific enough to drive improvement rather than merely describe impressions, and how to disaggregate board consensus from individual member observations when the board operates as a collective evaluator. These are learnable skills, and the study's findings suggest that boards are neither trained in them systematically nor provided with the ongoing development support that would build them over time.

Implications for Governance Practice

The cumulative picture this study presents is of a governance function that exists formally but operates poorly in practice — undermined by inadequate process design, insufficient training, and the informal relational dynamics that small governance bodies inevitably generate. Improving superintendent evaluation requires intervention at the process, training, and structural levels simultaneously.

  • Anchor evaluations in pre-established goals: Boards that set specific, measurable superintendent goals at the beginning of each performance period have something concrete to evaluate against. Boards that do not inevitably fall back on impressions, relationships, and vague assessments of general performance — none of which serve superintendent development.
  • Separate the evaluation conversation from the working relationship: Formal evaluation processes should include norms that distinguish the professional accountability review from the ongoing board-superintendent working relationship. This does not mean the relationship is irrelevant — it means it should not override performance evidence when the board is acting in its evaluative capacity.
  • Invest in board training before the evaluation cycle: Professional development in evaluation techniques should happen at the start of a board's term and before each annual evaluation cycle, not after problems emerge. State associations should treat superintendent evaluation training as a core governance competency, not an optional add-on.
  • Build in structured feedback mechanisms: Boards should use structured feedback formats that require specific, evidence-referenced observations rather than overall ratings. Narrative feedback tied to goal performance gives superintendents actionable information; numerical ratings without narrative context do not.
  • Evaluate the evaluation process itself: Boards should periodically review whether their superintendent evaluation practices are improving over time — not just whether they completed the annual review. This meta-level attention to process quality is itself a governance competency that the study suggests most boards have not yet developed.
The Bottom Line

Henrikson's study documents a persistent and consequential governance failure: school boards understand that evaluating the superintendent is their primary responsibility, yet they lack the training, consistent processes, and structural safeguards needed to do it well. The board-superintendent relationship compounds the problem by introducing social dynamics that can override professional accountability. For practitioners, the remedies are concrete — structured goal-setting, evaluator training, and deliberate process design — but they require boards and the organizations that support them to treat evaluation quality as an ongoing investment rather than an annual compliance exercise.
